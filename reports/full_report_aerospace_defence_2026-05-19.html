What Happened This Week

Canada's defence industry is entering one of its biggest spending cycles in modern history. The federal government this year crossed the threshold of spending 2% of the country's economic output on defence — a commitment worth more than $63 billion — and has now promised to reach 5% by 2035, a level no major Western nation currently meets. For the roughly 70,000 Canadians who work in aerospace and defence manufacturing from Montreal to Winnipeg to Prince Edward Island, that money translates into new contracts, new facilities, and new jobs: this week alone, a government investment of $110.7 million secured 120 new skilled positions at MDS Coating Technologies in P.E.I., while Ottawa-area defence tech firm J-Squared Technologies opened a new facility in Kanata. The challenge for Canadian businesses is moving fast enough to win contracts in a procurement environment that is accelerating globally — allied countries including Germany and Australia are also spending aggressively, and not all of them will wait for Canadian suppliers to get organised.

Intelligence Overview

Canada's crossing of the NATO 2% GDP defence spending threshold in March 2026 — confirmed this week as a $63 billion annual expenditure figure — is not a political milestone. It is a procurement trigger. Government modelling estimates that $18.3 billion in defence-related spending in 2025-26 alone will generate approximately 65,000 jobs and contribute $7.7 billion to GDP. The commitment to 5% of GDP by 2035, structured as 3.5% for core defence requirements and 1.5% for broader critical investments under the Hague Summit framework, means that Canadian aerospace and defence companies are looking at a sustained, multi-year capital programme expansion — not a single budget spike. The Defence Investment Agency (DIA), now centralising procurement expertise across NORAD modernisation and the Defence Industrial Strategy's six priority areas, is the institutional engine through which contracts will flow. Canadian firms not already registered on CanadaBuys.canada.ca and not already engaging DIA programme offices are already behind the procurement timeline on multiple streams.

This week's most commercially significant domestic event is the $110.7 million joint federal-provincial investment in MDS Coating Technologies' P.E.I. facility — the largest single private-sector investment in the province's history. The $55.7 million federal contribution matched by $55 million from the province supports a 50,000-square-foot expansion and up to 120 new skilled positions, doubling MDS's workforce. MDS already serves U.S. Marine Corps aircraft programmes, and this expansion positions Canada as a serious advanced coatings supplier to allied military aviation — a capability directly relevant to ITB obligations under the F-35 programme and to MRO work on platforms including the CH-148 Cyclone. For Nova Scotia and P.E.I.-based supply chain operators, MDS's growth creates downstream subcontracting opportunities in surface treatment, quality assurance, and materials logistics.

In the space and surveillance domain, MDA Space continues to demonstrate that Canadian firms can win on merit in a competitive defence technology market. Q1 2026 revenues of $464.1 million — up 31.8% year-over-year — and a $32 million contract from the DIA for three Ground-Based Optical observatories for space surveillance, combined with the April launch of the MDA MIDNIGHT space control platform, confirm MDA as a Tier-1 prime in the Space Domain Awareness stream of NORAD modernisation. The Telesat-MDA pairing on the Enhanced Satellite Communications Project – Polar (ESCP-P), following the December 2025 $2.92 million initial contract for engineering and options analysis, signals that Canada is building a domestically anchored Arctic military satellite communications architecture. Suppliers with RF, ground systems, or satellite operations capabilities should be engaging MDA and Telesat procurement teams now — ITB obligations will require Canadian sourcing as the programme scales.

Alied signals carry mixed implications for Canadian exporters this week. The $991 million in F-35 modification contracts awarded May 15, including $547.8 million in combined FMS funding for allied nations, confirms a robust DSCA Foreign Military Sales pipeline that Canadian suppliers under the Defence Production Sharing Agreement (DPSA) can access. AUKUS Pillar II vendor qualification activity in Australia — with first contracts to HIFraser, Mack Valves, Bale Defence, and STAUFF Corporation — establishes the template Canadian suppliers must match to enter UK and U.S. submarine supply chains. Conversely, Germany's Bundeswehr Planning and Procurement Acceleration Act, passed January 15, creates a structural risk: it permits exclusion of non-EU companies without justification, directly threatening Canadian suppliers' ability to access Germany's €83 billion annual defence budget despite zero-tariff access under the Canada-EU Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement (CETA). Canadian firms with German market aspirations need legal counsel on compliance strategies before pursuing Bundeswehr tenders.

What We Think
The pattern beneath this week's signals is one of Canadian defence procurement reaching an institutional inflection point, not just a budget inflection point. Crossing 2% GDP and committing to 5% would mean little if the procurement machinery couldn't execute. The establishment of the Defence Investment Agency, the selection of Telesat and MDA as ESCP-P primes, MDA's $32 million SDA contract, and the $110.7 million MDS investment all point to a government that is actively building Canadian industrial champions rather than reflexively routing contracts through foreign primes with ITB afterthoughts. This is a deliberate industrial policy shift embedded inside a defence spending surge, and it has commercial consequences that most Canadian SMEs have not yet registered. What Canadian businesses are most likely getting wrong is the speed requirement. The NATO counter-drone marketplace selects 18 systems with contracts by summer 2026 — that window is measured in weeks, not quarters. The ITAR-to-EAR transfer of Category IV and XV space items, implemented in late 2024, has already expanded license exceptions for space technology to 40 countries including Canada under new ECCN codes, but companies that haven't reclassified their products and updated their export compliance systems are carrying unnecessary regulatory drag. Germany's procurement exclusion law is live now, not prospective, and Canadian firms without a legal strategy to demonstrate interoperability or EU-qualifying content face direct exclusion from the world's second-largest NATO spender. These are not things to add to a Q3 planning agenda. The evidence points toward a 60-day window in which the Canadian firms that move — registering for NATO marketplace, engaging DIA on ESCP-P and NORAD stream milestones, restructuring their export compliance for post-EAR-revision space products, and securing AUKUS Pillar II vendor qualification positioning through Five Eyes channels — will establish competitive positions that latecomers cannot buy their way into. Canada's defence industrial base has rarely had this alignment of domestic spending, allied demand, and institutional support. The firms that treat this week's signals as a starting gun rather than background noise will look back at spring 2026 as the moment they captured a generational market position.

Brings together multiple StatCan datasets in a single country-level analytical tool.
  • ISED Canadian Importers Database (2023 (CBSA-sourced) — NOTE: typically lags ~2 years; 2024 not yet released as of April 2026) — Company-level list of major Canadian importers by product (HS code), city, and country of origin. 'Major importers' = companies collectively accounting for up to 80% of imports of a given product. Includes customs brokers and non-resident importers.
  • ISED Trade Data Online (Monthly — current through recent months) — Monthly aggregate bilateral trade flows for Canadian imports and exports, by HS product and NAICS industry, for 200+ countries. Sourced from Statistics Canada and U.S. Census Bureau.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's State of Trade (2024 full-year (2025 report)) — Annual comprehensive report on Canada's international trade and economic performance. Covers merchandise exports/imports, services trade, FDI flows in and out, sector-by-sector performance, and the economic outlook. 2025 edition covers full-year 2024 data.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's Response to U.S. Tariffs (Current — actively updated) — Live policy page covering Canada's response to U.S. tariffs including relief measures, support programs, retaliatory measures, and sector impacts. Frequently updated — key cross-sector risk signal.
  • CanadaBuys — Government of Canada Procurement (Real-time) — Official Government of Canada procurement portal. Covers federal tender notices, awarded contracts, and standing offers. Key for Aerospace & Defence and Tech sectors.
