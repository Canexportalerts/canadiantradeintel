Housing & Infrastructure

Housing, infrastructure, and the construction supply chain. The domestic investment story, and the gap between what Canada has committed to build and its current capacity to build it.

Research brief · Q2 2026 Updated April 2026 Canadian Trade Intelligence Inc.
CTI Position
Canada's housing shortfall is not a demand management problem. It is a supply capacity problem compounded by a productivity crisis and a fiscal commitment gap. The 500,000 homes per year target is the right ambition. The current trajectory, 259,000 starts declining through 2028, and federal programme spending falling 56% by 2028–29, is not. The construction supply chain needs the same infrastructure-investment logic applied to transit and energy: predictable demand signals, sovereign material inputs, and technology adoption supported by evidence rather than optimism. Until Build Canada Homes scales from a 4,000-unit pilot into a funded national programme, and until the softwood lumber dispute is resolved or hedged, the gap between rhetoric and construction reality will define this policy file.
Analysis

Canada needs to build at a scale it has never built before

Canada's housing and infrastructure deficit is structural, not cyclical. The Carney government has committed to doubling residential construction to 500,000 homes per year over the next decade. Canada currently builds approximately 259,000 units per year, and the Spring 2026 CMHC Housing Supply Report projects starts will decline through 2026 to 2028 as condominium presales collapse and developers face elevated costs. The gap between the stated target and the current trajectory is not a rounding error. It is the central domestic economic challenge of this decade.

The productivity problem is where the research is most candid. The Centre for the Study of Living Standards estimates that residential construction productivity losses between 2019 and 2024 added between $6 and $8 billion to housing construction costs in Canada, accounting for up to 20% of the increase in new home prices over that period. In 2024, residential construction accounted for 4.2% of business-sector employment but only 3.3% of business-sector value added. The industry is expanding its workforce while producing less output per worker. This is the definition of a productivity crisis, and no volume of new housing starts will solve it without confronting it directly.

The most important number in Canadian housing policy right now is not 500,000. It is 56%. Federal housing programme spending is projected to decline 56% between 2025–26 and 2028–29, from $9.8 billion to $4.3 billion, as existing programme funding expires and new programmes remain at pilot scale. The Carney government's Build Canada Homes initiative is currently scoped to support approximately 4,000 new homes — a pilot, not a system. The fiscal commitment does not yet match the stated ambition. That gap is either a future budget story or evidence that the 500,000 target is aspirational rather than funded policy. Both possibilities matter for every business in the construction supply chain planning capacity investments.

Signal Log

What CTI is watching

May 2026  ·  Watch: Federal Budget
2026–27 budget housing envelope reversal or confirmation
The single most important signal for this file. Does federal housing programme spending reverse the projected 56% decline, or does Build Canada Homes remain a pilot? If the envelope expands materially, the 500,000 target becomes credible. If it doesn't, the gap between stated ambition and funded policy is confirmed. Watch the autumn fiscal update and any spring budget announcement.
May 2026  ·  Watch: CUSMA Review
Softwood lumber CVD outcome — July 2026 formal window
The July 2026 CUSMA joint review is the formal window on the 14.54% countervailing duty on Canadian softwood lumber. A resolution that reduces or eliminates the duty would lower residential construction costs across North America and improve Canadian lumber export economics. An escalation would embed higher costs across every residential project budget in both countries. BC producers filed for Chapter 19 dispute panel review in January 2026; outcome expected Q3 2026 at earliest.
May 2026  ·  Watch: Zoning Reform
Municipal as-of-right zoning adoption pace
Federal housing funding is increasingly conditional on municipalities adopting as-of-right zoning for density near transit. The pace at which large municipalities — Toronto most watched — actually amend their zoning bylaws is the operational measure of whether the policy is translating into supply. The delay between federal conditions and municipal implementation is the most predictable constraint on near-term starts growth.
May 2026  ·  Watch: ACLP
Apartment Construction Loan Program committed vs. disbursed capital ratio
The ACLP's $55 billion envelope is real capital, but deployment depends on developers having viable projects at current construction costs and interest rates. CMHC's quarterly reports on approval rates and the ratio of committed to disbursed capital will show whether the financing instrument is reaching the projects that need it or whether cost and rate conditions are preventing uptake at scale.

Key Researchers

Voices shaping this conversation

Smart Prosperity Institute · University of Ottawa
Mike Moffatt
Moffatt's work on housing supply is the most practically grounded analysis available on why Canada cannot build at the rate it needs to. His research documents the specific zoning restrictions, development charge structures, and regulatory fragmentation that constrain supply regardless of federal funding. His 2024 and 2025 work for CMHC's Housing Supply Challenge informed the government's Housing Design Catalogue and the as-of-right zoning reform conditions attached to federal housing funding. His finding that missing middle housing near transit delivers the highest affordability per dollar of public investment is now embedded in federal policy frameworks.
smartprosperity.ca →
School of Cities · University of Toronto
Cheryl Case and the City Building TMU Research Group
The School of Cities and City Building TMU team's modelling for the Task Force for Housing and Climate provides the most rigorous analysis of what type of housing Canada should be building, not just how much. Their March 2024 Blueprint for More and Better Housing identified the trade-offs between supply volume, affordability, climate compatibility, and location, finding that the mix of housing types built matters as much as the total number of units. Their work is essential context for any business operating in construction technology, modular building, or mass timber, as it identifies which product types have the strongest policy demand signal.
schoolofcities.utoronto.ca →
C.D. Howe Institute
Dragicevic, Riaz and colleagues
The C.D. Howe Institute's July 2025 analysis "Building Smarter, Faster" is the most recent rigorous examination of construction technology adoption barriers in Canada. Their key finding: innovative construction methods like modular, mass timber, and 3D concrete printing face not a technology gap but a policy and evidence gap. Canadian decision-makers lack the country-specific cost and outcome data to confidently deploy these methods at scale. The study calls for academic-industry partnerships to generate this evidence, a finding that has direct implications for procurement strategy and for which construction innovations have the strongest near-term adoption case.
