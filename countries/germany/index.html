Germany is Canada's most significant bilateral trading partner within the European Union — though the relationship is heavily import-weighted and the Canadian export trend is moving in the wrong direction. Canadian exports to Germany fell 9.6% in 2024, among the sharpest declines of any major trading partner, driven primarily by a pullback in metal ores and non-metallic minerals. This is partly cyclical — Germany's industrial economy contracted through much of 2023–2024 — but it also reflects structural underperformance in Canadian CETA utilization.

Canada imports approximately $23.8B worth of goods from Germany annually, concentrated in motor vehicles (BMW, Mercedes, Volkswagen group), industrial machinery, pharmaceuticals, and chemicals. The trade deficit reflects Germany's position as a leading exporter of high-value industrial products rather than any particular trade barrier — CETA has eliminated tariffs in both directions for most goods.

Key Canadian exports to Germany: Metal ores and concentrates, aircraft and aerospace components, pharmaceuticals, agri-food products (canola, pulses), precious metals, and machinery.

Key Canadian imports from Germany: Motor vehicles and auto parts, industrial machinery, pharmaceutical products, chemicals and plastics, electronic equipment, and medical devices.

⚑ WATCH
Canadian export volumes to Germany have declined two consecutive years. The immediate driver is German industrial weakness — GDP contracted in 2024 and manufacturing output fell. But the deeper issue is CETA underutilization: Canadian exporters have not yet fully exploited the tariff-free access CETA provides. The opportunity gap is widest in agri-food, cleantech, and professional services. See CETA guide →
02

Market Opportunity

Germany's Energiewende — its legally mandated transition away from coal, nuclear, and ultimately fossil fuels — is the defining economic story of the next decade for Canadian exporters. The programme requires massive investment in renewable energy infrastructure, grid modernization, energy storage, and clean industrial inputs. Canada is uniquely positioned to supply several critical components of this transition.

Critical Minerals & Cleantech
Germany's battery manufacturing sector (Northvolt Gigafactory, BASF cathode materials, VW PowerCo) has urgent demand for lithium, cobalt, nickel, and graphite. Under the EU Critical Raw Materials Act, Canadian producers are explicitly targeted as preferred non-Chinese supply sources. This is the highest-potential bilateral opportunity of the next five years.
Hydrogen & Clean Energy
Germany has committed to importing green hydrogen at scale — it cannot produce sufficient domestic supply. Canada's hydrogen strategy targets Germany as a primary export destination. The Canada-Germany Hydrogen Alliance signed in 2022 established a framework for large-scale hydrogen trade, with the first deliveries targeted for the mid-2020s.
Aerospace & Defence
Germany is significantly increasing defence spending toward and beyond the NATO 2% GDP target — from a position of historic underinvestment. Canadian aerospace and defence companies have procurement opportunities in air defence, surveillance, and advanced manufacturing supply chains. The Bombardier–Lufthansa relationship is a reference point for the bilateral aerospace commercial relationship.
Agri-food & Premium Goods
Germany is the EU's largest food market. CETA has eliminated tariffs on most Canadian agri-food products, but Canadian exporters have captured only a fraction of their potential market share. Premium Canadian products — maple syrup, seafood, organic grains, specialty pulses — have identifiable German consumer demand that is undersupplied by current Canadian export volumes.
Government Procurement
CETA's government procurement chapter gives Canadian companies the right to bid on German federal and state government contracts above defined thresholds. German public procurement spending is substantial — infrastructure, IT systems, research equipment, and professional services are all accessible to Canadian bidders who engage actively.
Technology & Digital Services
Germany's Mittelstand (mid-sized industrial companies) is undergoing significant digital transformation. Canadian enterprise software, industrial AI, and cybersecurity firms have identifiable market opportunities — Germany's manufacturing sector is actively seeking technology partners for Industry 4.0 adoption.
03

Investment Climate

Germany offers one of the world's most stable and legally predictable investment environments — EU rule of law, strong intellectual property protection, deep capital markets, and an independent judiciary. The World Bank and major multilateral indices consistently rank Germany in the top tier for investor protection and contract enforcement.

The most significant near-term concern for Canadian investors is the German economic environment itself. Germany's reliance on cheap Russian energy — eliminated by the Ukraine conflict — has exposed structural vulnerabilities in its industrial model. Energy costs have risen substantially, and several major industrial companies have announced production shifts toward lower-cost jurisdictions, including Canada. This creates both risk (weaker German economy = weaker demand for Canadian exports) and opportunity (German industrial investment in Canada is growing).

Inbound German investment in Canada has been growing in renewable energy (Siemens, Nordex wind energy installations), automotive supply chains (VW battery gigafactory in St. Thomas, Ontario), and financial services. These flows represent Canadian assets attracting capital rather than bilateral trade, but they strengthen the overall relationship and create procurement networks.

⚑ WATCH
The Volkswagen battery gigafactory commitment in St. Thomas, Ontario — announced 2023 with federal and provincial support — is the single largest German investment in Canada in modern history and signals a structural shift in the bilateral relationship toward clean energy supply chains. 04

Risk Profile

Germany presents low geopolitical and regulatory risk for Canadian businesses — it is a rules-based, democratic, EU-governed market with strong contract enforcement. The principal risks are economic and structural rather than political or security-related.

Risk Category Level Assessment
Political stability Low Coalition government standard; policy continuity strong. 2025 federal election produced stable CDU-led coalition. EU membership provides additional stability anchor.
Regulatory complexity Moderate Germany's regulatory environment is thorough and predictable but dense — particularly in environmental, labour, and data protection. GDPR compliance is mandatory for digital services. CETA reduces but does not eliminate regulatory friction.
Economic risk Moderate German GDP contracted in 2024; manufacturing output fell sharply. Energy transition costs and loss of cheap Russian gas have structurally repriced German industrial competitiveness. Recovery trajectory is uncertain heading into 2025–26.
Currency risk Low–Moderate EUR/CAD volatility is manageable for most trade transactions. Euro is a major reserve currency with deep hedging markets. ECB monetary policy has stabilized after 2022–23 rate cycle.
Supply chain risk Low Germany has sophisticated logistics infrastructure. No significant supply chain chokepoints specific to Canada–Germany trade. North Sea and Rotterdam port access is reliable.
Geopolitical / security Low No direct security risk. Germany's proximity to Ukraine conflict has increased defence awareness but not created operational risk for commercial activity. NATO membership provides collective security guarantee.
05

Trade Agreement Coverage

Canada–Germany trade operates primarily under CETA — the most comprehensive trade agreement Canada has outside of North America. CETA gives Canadian exporters and investors legal certainty, tariff-free access for nearly all goods, and the right to bid on German government procurement contracts.

CETA In Force — Provisionally Applied
Comprehensive Economic and Trade Agreement between Canada and the European Union. Applied provisionally since September 21, 2017. By 2024, 98.8% of tariff lines on Canadian goods entering Germany are duty-free. CETA also opens German federal and state procurement contracts to Canadian bidders.
What this means for Canadian businesses in Germany: Your goods likely enter Germany tariff-free — verify your HS code using the Canada Tariff Finder. Confirm you meet CETA rules of origin (sufficient Canadian content). Check the EU TED procurement database for German government contracts you are now legally eligible to bid on.
Important caveat: CETA is still "provisionally applied" — the investment protection chapter (ISDS) is not yet in full force pending ratification by all EU member states. Core goods and services access is unaffected.

Beyond CETA, Canada and Germany are both WTO members, both G7 members, and both parties to NATO — layers of institutional relationship that support commercial engagement even where specific trade law does not reach.

06

Canadian Government Signals

The Canadian government has signalled Germany as a priority bilateral relationship through multiple policy instruments — both explicitly in strategy documents and implicitly through program commitments and diplomatic investment. These signals matter because they indicate where Canadian government support (TCS assistance, EDC financing, CanExport funding) is most actively flowing.

📄
Canada–Germany Hydrogen Alliance · August 2022
Canada and Germany signed a Joint Declaration of Intent on hydrogen cooperation during Chancellor Scholz's visit to Canada — the first agreement of its kind between the two countries. The alliance commits both governments to enabling large-scale hydrogen trade, with Canada as supplier and Germany as importer, targeting first deliveries in the mid-2020s.
📄
CETA Implementation — Global Affairs Canada · Ongoing
Germany is one of Canada's primary targets for CETA utilization improvement. The Trade Commissioner Service maintains offices in Berlin and Frankfurt, with explicit mandates to support Canadian businesses entering the German market and accessing EU procurement. Canada has identified CETA underutilization — particularly in agri-food and services — as a priority policy problem.
📄
Critical Minerals Strategy — Natural Resources Canada · 2022
Canada's Critical Minerals Strategy explicitly identifies the European Union — and Germany in particular — as a priority market for Canadian critical minerals exports, aligned with the EU's own Critical Raw Materials Act. NRCan has prioritized German partnerships for lithium, cobalt, and nickel supply chain development.
📄
VW Battery Gigafactory — Federal Investment Support · 2023
The Canadian federal government committed significant incentive support for the Volkswagen PowerCo battery gigafactory in St. Thomas, Ontario — the largest automotive investment in Canadian history at up to $7B CAD. This signals at the highest policy level that Canada views German industrial investment as strategically important and is willing to compete aggressively for it.

For businesses seeking Canadian government support for Germany market entry: contact the Trade Commissioner Service Berlin office, check CanExport SME eligibility for Germany-bound market development activities, and speak with Export Development Canada about financing instruments available for the German market.

07

Procurement Pipeline

CETA's government procurement chapter gives Canadian companies the legal right to bid on German federal government contracts and contracts from Länder (state) governments above defined financial thresholds. This is one of the most underused provisions in Canadian trade law — most Canadian businesses have never explored German public procurement despite having full legal access.

Where to find opportunities: The EU Tenders Electronic Daily (TED) database publishes all above-threshold EU procurement notices, including German federal and state contracts. The TCS Global Bid Opportunity Finder filters for opportunities accessible to Canadian businesses under CETA.

Active procurement sectors in Germany (2024–2026): Defence and security (Bundeswehr modernization programme), digital infrastructure (federal IT modernization), energy infrastructure (grid expansion and storage), transport (rail and EV charging network), and public health technology.

⚑ WATCH
Germany's Bundeswehr (armed forces) modernization programme — funded by the €100B special defence fund announced in 2022 — represents the largest procurement opportunity Canada has had in the German market in decades.
