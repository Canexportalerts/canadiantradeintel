Overview

Sweden is Canada's seventh-largest EU bilateral trade partner, with total goods trade of approximately $3B CAD in 2024. The bilateral relationship is characterized by high-value, knowledge-intensive trade in both directions: Canada exports minerals and raw materials while Sweden exports advanced manufactured goods, vehicles (Volvo Cars, Volvo Trucks), and sophisticated technology products. CETA provides the legal framework for zero-tariff goods trade across the relationship.

The transformative dimension of the Canada–Sweden relationship is the emerging critical minerals and battery supply chain nexus. Sweden hosts Northvolt, Europe's largest EV battery producer (though undergoing restructuring in 2024–2025), and LKAB, Europe's largest iron ore producer and a global leader in developing zero-carbon iron ore processing. Sweden's mining sector has deep technical expertise in iron ore, base metals, and industrial minerals processing — directly complementary to Canada's position as one of the world's largest critical minerals producers. Canada's recently expanded critical minerals partnerships under the Trans-Atlantic framework and G7 Critical Minerals Action Plan explicitly identify Sweden as a processing and manufacturing partner.

Ulf Kristersson's centre-right government, in power since October 2022 and supported in parliament by the Sweden Democrats (nationalist-conservative), has pursued pragmatic economic and security policies. Sweden's NATO accession on March 7, 2024 — completing the Nordic-Baltic NATO perimeter — represents the most significant Swedish security decision in over two centuries of military non-alignment. NATO membership elevates Sweden's importance as a defence procurement partner for Canada and creates new bilateral military-industrial cooperation opportunities.

For Canadian businesses, Sweden's government is stable, business-friendly, and highly transparent — Sweden consistently ranks in the top five globally for ease of doing business, rule of law, and anti-corruption metrics. The Kristersson government's emphasis on Swedish industrial competitiveness, defence spending, and nuclear energy restoral (reversing previous phase-out decisions) creates clear commercial signals for Canadian companies in these sectors.

Sweden's economy contracted in 2023 and grew only modestly in 2024, impacted by a housing market correction (some of Europe's most interest-rate-sensitive household balance sheets), weak German export demand, and global manufacturing headwinds. Sweden's long-run economic fundamentals remain strong: triple-A sovereign credit, a globally competitive industrial base in automotive, telecom (Ericsson), defence (SAAB), and life sciences (AstraZeneca, Essity), and a highly educated, flexible workforce. Recovery is forecast for 2025 as Riksbank (Sweden's central bank) cuts interest rates and housing market distress recedes.

Sweden is not a eurozone member — it retains the Swedish Krona (SEK) — giving monetary policy independence. SEK weakened significantly through 2023–2024, reducing Swedish export prices in foreign markets but creating some currency risk for Canadian companies with SEK-denominated revenues in Sweden. The Riksbank's rate-cutting cycle in 2024–2025 is expected to stabilize SEK.

Top Canadian exports to Sweden: Mineral products — iron ore, copper concentrates, potash, and nickel — which feed Sweden's steel and industrial metals processing sector; wood products and pulp (Sweden and Canada are complementary forest products producers but Sweden also imports some Canadian specialty grades); agricultural commodities; and aerospace components for Swedish aerospace supply chains.

Top Canadian imports from Sweden: Volvo Cars and Volvo Trucks — Volvo is one of Canada's most significant Swedish import categories by value; IKEA (Swedish parent, products sourced globally but brand drives significant Canada retail spend); pharmaceutical and medical products (AstraZeneca Swedish R&D, Essity healthcare products); precision industrial equipment and tools; and technology/telecom equipment (Ericsson network infrastructure, though Canada's 5G network decisions limited Ericsson's Canadian market).

Market Access

CETA Provisionally Applied — September 21, 2017
CETA governs Canada–Sweden goods trade with zero tariffs on approximately 98% of EU tariff lines. Sweden ratified CETA through the EU Council process. Swedish market standards are set at EU level but Sweden has a reputation for strict interpretation of EU product safety and environmental standards — Canadian goods must meet EU standards which Sweden enforces rigorously. Sweden's domestic market is highly transparent and open — procurement is conducted under EU Public Procurement Directives with strong anti-corruption enforcement.
Services note: Sweden's professional services market (engineering, law, finance) is regulated through Swedish professional bodies that implement EU mutual recognition directives — CETA's professional qualifications provisions apply, facilitating Canadian professional services delivery in Sweden with more streamlined recognition than third-country providers face.

06

Opportunity Assessment

Critical Minerals — STRONG
Sweden has world-class lithium-ion battery processing capacity through Northvolt (restructuring post-2024 financial difficulties but strategically important) and LKAB's HYBRIT green steel processing. Canadian critical minerals producers — nickel, lithium, cobalt — are natural supply chain partners for Sweden's battery and green steel industries. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act creates regulatory pull for Canadian-sourced materials.
Advanced Manufacturing — STRONG
Swedish industrial machinery, precision tools, and specialty materials companies are global leaders and have historically sourced Canadian raw material inputs. Volvo's Canadian dealer network creates reverse supply chain opportunities for Canadian auto parts suppliers. Swedish engineering firms' Canadian-market expansion creates bilateral partnerships in advanced manufacturing.
Aerospace & Defence — EMERGING
SAAB's Gripen fighter aircraft was evaluated (not selected) in Canada's fighter replacement competition, but SAAB's defence industrial capabilities are relevant for Canadian defence supply chain partnerships in areas including radar systems, electronic warfare, and military aircraft maintenance. Sweden's rapidly rising defence budget (targeting 2.6% GDP) creates procurement pipeline growth.
Technology & Digital — EMERGING
Sweden has produced a disproportionate number of global tech companies per capita (Spotify, Klarna, King, Mojang). Canadian technology companies seeking EU market entry often partner with Swedish tech firms that share similar regulatory and corporate culture environments. Stockholm's startup ecosystem is directly comparable to Toronto and Vancouver in scale and sophistication.
WATCH
Northvolt restructuring impact: Northvolt's 2024–2025 financial restructuring (Chapter 11 bankruptcy filing in the US, Swedish restructuring proceedings) creates uncertainty for its Canadian critical minerals supply chain commitments — but also potential acquisition opportunities for Canadian capital seeking European battery processing assets. LKAB HYBRIT green iron: LKAB's HYBRIT process for hydrogen-reduced iron ore (eliminating coking coal) could create a new market for Canadian hydrogen exports to Sweden's steel sector — monitor commercialization timeline.
07

Canadian Business Presence

Bombardier has supplied rail equipment and aircraft components to Swedish operators, including Stockholm's metro system and connections with SAAB aerospace supply chains. Canadian pension funds (CPPIB, Ontario Teachers') have Swedish infrastructure exposure through European diversified mandates — Swedish infrastructure (ports, energy distribution) is a pension fund investment category. Manulife and Sun Life have Swedish customers through European insurance platforms.

Canadian mining companies with critical minerals production — particularly Teck Resources (before its reorganization), First Quantum, and various TSX-listed nickel and lithium producers — have developed or are developing supply relationships with Swedish battery and steel manufacturers. The bilateral critical minerals relationship is at an early stage with significant growth potential. Several Canadian cleantech companies have established Stockholm offices as their EU market entry point, leveraging Sweden's English-language business environment, rule of law, and EU single market access.

The Swedish-Canadian Chamber of Commerce maintains active bilateral business promotion, particularly in cleantech, life sciences, and advanced manufacturing sectors where Swedish and Canadian companies have natural commercial complementarity.

Risk Register

Political Low Sweden is among the world's most stable democracies — rule of law, transparency, and policy predictability are exceptional. The Kristersson government's Sweden Democrat support creates some immigration policy controversy but has zero effect on trade and investment policy.
Regulatory Low Sweden rigorously enforces EU product standards and environmental regulations — Canadian goods must be fully EU-compliant. Swedish public procurement is among Europe's most transparent. Regulatory costs are predictable and consistently applied.

Geopolitical Low Sweden's NATO membership (since March 2024) fully integrates it into the Western security alliance — Russia remains a background security concern for Swedish planning but poses no direct commercial risk to Canadian businesses operating in Sweden.
Market Access Low Sweden's market is genuinely open — no significant informal barriers to foreign companies. English is near-universally spoken in Swedish business, making Sweden one of the most accessible EU markets for English-speaking Canadian companies. Low corruption and strong contract enforcement.
09

Procurement Pipeline

Sweden's public procurement market is conducted through Upphandlingsmyndigheten (UHM) — the Swedish Agency for Public Procurement — and published on the national electronic platform (e-Avrop and TendSign). Under CETA's government procurement chapter, Canadian companies are eligible to bid on above-threshold Swedish central government and selected sub-central entity tenders. Sweden's procurement is known for its transparency and rigour — decisions are legally challengeable and procurement authorities take compliance seriously.

🔗
Swedish Defence Procurement — FMV (Försvarets materielverk)
Sweden's Defence Materiel Administration (FMV) manages Swedish defence procurement. With Sweden's defence budget rising toward 2.6% of GDP, FMV procurement pipeline growth is substantial — covering Gripen aircraft sustainment, naval systems, artillery, and C4ISR. Canadian defence companies with NATO-interoperable products should monitor FMV procurement signals and explore industrial partnership with SAAB as the Swedish prime contractor.
🔗
LKAB — Green Transition Procurement
LKAB's HYBRIT green steel initiative (with SSAB and Vattenfall) involves substantial supply chain procurement for hydrogen, carbon capture, and zero-emission mining technology. Canadian companies with hydrogen technology, mining equipment, and clean energy expertise should monitor LKAB procurement through its supplier portal and Vattenfall's green energy procurement activities.
🔗
TCS Sweden — Stockholm
The Trade Commissioner Service maintains an office in Stockholm covering advanced manufacturing, cleantech, critical minerals, defence, and life sciences. Sweden is one of TCS's most actively programmed EU markets due to the depth of bilateral commercial complementarity.

Government Signals

📄
G7 Critical Minerals Action Plan — Sweden as Processing Partner
Canada's G7 Critical Minerals Action Plan and the broader Trans-Atlantic minerals partnership explicitly identify Sweden as a key critical minerals processing and manufacturing partner. Swedish battery and steel processing capacity aligns with Canadian raw materials production in a supply chain model that reduces dependence on Chinese processing — a core policy objective for both governments.
📄
NATO Membership — New Defence Partnership Opportunities
Sweden's March 2024 NATO accession opens formal NATO industrial co-operation channels between Canadian and Swedish defence companies for the first time. Under NATO defence procurement frameworks, Canadian and Swedish companies can pursue joint programmes, technology sharing, and co-production arrangements not previously available under Sweden's non-aligned status.
📄
CETA Joint Committee — Cleantech and Critical Minerals Dialogue
The Canada–EU CETA Joint Committee's working group on clean energy and critical minerals has specifically identified Canada–Sweden supply chain development as a priority area — creating government-level support for Canadian critical minerals exporters developing Swedish processing partnerships.
WATCH
Swedish nuclear restoral: The Kristersson government has reversed Sweden's nuclear phase-out policy and is pursuing new nuclear capacity construction — Canadian uranium producers and nuclear technology companies (notably Cameco, with existing partnerships) should monitor Swedish nuclear procurement signals. Ericsson 5G in Canada: Canada excluded Ericsson and Nokia from its 5G security concerns in late 2023 — Ericsson remains eligible for Canadian 5G network buildout, creating a reciprocal technology trade opportunity.
