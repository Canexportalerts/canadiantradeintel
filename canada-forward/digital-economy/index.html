AI Adoption, Compute Infrastructure, and Regulatory Sovereignty

Digital Economy and Data Sovereignty

Canada contributed C$223 billion in digital GDP in 2024, leads the G7 in AI research, and holds the lowest publicly available AI compute infrastructure of any G7 country. Only 12% of Canadian businesses use AI to deliver services. The gap between what Canada has built in research and what it has captured commercially is the central digital economy policy challenge of the decade.

Research brief · Q2 2026  |  Updated April 2026  |  Canadian Trade Intelligence Inc.
CTI Position

Canada's best path forward in the digital economy is not to try to out-compete US platforms on their own terms. It is to build a distinct Canadian position: sovereign compute infrastructure, robust data governance that ensures Canadian data assets generate Canadian economic value, strong AI commercialization pathways that keep IP ownership in Canada, and a digital trade posture through CETA and CPTPP that opens markets for Canadian software and digital services companies. The AI productivity opportunity is real — if 10% of Canadian work hours are meaningfully augmented by AI, a conservative 2% GDP lift follows. But capturing that productivity gain requires Canadian businesses to actually adopt AI, which requires compute access, clear governance frameworks, and a policy environment that does not make digital investment more complex than necessary.

Canada leads in AI research and is falling behind in everything that follows

Canada's digital economy contributed C$223.45 billion to GDP in 2024, with 12% of the Canadian workforce employed in digital sectors and the ICTC projecting C$249.65 billion by 2030. Canada was the first country in the world to introduce a national AI strategy, in 2017, and has produced world-class AI research through the Vector Institute, Mila, and the Alberta Machine Intelligence Institute. And yet, as of late 2025, only approximately 12% of Canadian businesses report using AI to produce or deliver services, and Canada holds the lowest amount of publicly available computing infrastructure and performance of any G7 country. The gap between research excellence and commercial application is one of the most consequential structural weaknesses in the Canadian economy.

The sovereignty dimension of this gap is not rhetorical. Canada faces a binary choice between building the data infrastructure and compute capacity that would allow it to participate meaningfully in the AI era, and becoming a digital colony — subservient to AI infrastructure and large language models controlled by a small number of companies in Silicon Valley and Beijing. Canada cannot compete with the United States or China on foundational model development. The resources required, estimated at over US$100 billion per model generation, are simply unavailable in the Canadian public or private economy. But Canada does not need to compete on foundational models to have meaningful digital sovereignty. It needs to build compute infrastructure for inference and deployment, establish data governance frameworks that ensure Canadian data generates value in Canada rather than exporting it to US platforms, and develop the commercialization pathways that translate Canadian AI research into Canadian companies.

The Digital Services Tax episode illustrates the sovereignty challenge concisely. Canada enacted a 3% DST on the Canadian revenue of large digital platforms in 2024. The United States then launched a CUSMA dispute against the DST, framing what was effectively corporate lobbying by Meta, Google, Amazon, and Apple as a US national interest trade grievance. Under US pressure, the Carney government announced repeal of the DST in 2025, sacrificing C$7 billion in projected five-year revenue — and, more significantly, demonstrating that Canada's ability to independently regulate its digital economy is constrained by the political leverage that US-based platform companies can bring to bear through the trade relationship. The Online News Act, the Online Streaming Act, Bill C-27, and the DST repeal form a pattern: Canada attempts to regulate the digital economy in the Canadian public interest, and US-based platforms mobilize trade leverage to neutralize those regulations.

Signal Log

AI compute fund — first $800M grants issued FEB 2026

ISED issued the first tranche of the $2.4B AI compute fund — $800M in access credits for Canadian AI researchers and startups through the National AI Compute Access Program. Eligible companies receive subsidized access to Nvidia H100 clusters hosted in Canadian data centres. Watch: whether investment is in Canadian-owned or US-owned infrastructure determines whether IP stays in Canada.

Canada confirmed US Tier 1 AI export control designation JAN 2026

The US Bureau of Industry and Security confirmed Canada's Tier 1 designation under the AI diffusion rule, exempting Canada from chip export controls that apply to most other countries. Canadian AI companies retain unrestricted access to advanced Nvidia and AMD chips — a structural advantage over competitors in Tier 2 markets that should be actively leveraged for export positioning.

National AI institutes — $200M second phase announced MAR 2026

ISED announced $200M in second-phase funding for Vector Institute (Toronto), Mila (Montreal), and Amii (Edmonton) in March 2026. Funding includes a new commercialization mandate requiring each institute to track spinout company formation and industry licensing revenues annually — a signal that federal policy is shifting focus from research excellence to commercial capture.

Bill C-27 data protection — re-introduction watch WATCH

Federal data protection legislation stalled through multiple parliamentary sessions. Any re-introduction will define the legal operating environment for Canadian digital businesses and Canadian data rights. Canada's current privacy framework predates the smartphone. Quebec's updated law has been in force since 2022. The federal gap constrains Canadian AI development and remains the single most significant digital governance deficit.

Key Researchers

Policy Options · IRPP
Natasha Tusikov, Blayne Haggart, and Guillaume Beaumier

Policy Options has published the most sustained and rigorous academic analysis of Canada's digital sovereignty challenges. Tusikov and Haggart's November 2025 piece on AI risks to Canada's strategy documents the Cohere deal's sovereignty limitations and the structural pattern of US platform companies using trade mechanisms to neutralize Canadian digital regulation. Beaumier's May 2025 analysis of Canadian digital sovereignty provides the broader governance framework. Their work is the essential context for understanding why the DST repeal, the stalled C-27 legislation, and the compute infrastructure debate matter beyond their immediate policy contexts.

Policy Magazine
Digital Sovereignty and the Compute Economy (January 2026)

Policy Magazine's January 2026 analysis articulates the strongest available case for Canada as a middle-power AI leader through a compute sovereignty strategy rather than a foundational model competition strategy. The distinction is critical: rather than competing with the US on a battlefield where Canada cannot win, the analysis proposes that Canada build sovereign compute infrastructure, establish data embassy frameworks for allied nations processing sensitive data under Canadian law, and leverage Canada's clean energy grid as a competitive advantage for the energy-intensive AI inference workloads that drive commercial AI value.

Information and Communications Technology Council
Digital Economy Roadmap to 2030 (April 2025)

ICTC's April 2025 Roadmap provides the most comprehensive quantitative baseline for Canada's digital economy: C$223 billion in 2024 GDP, 12% workforce share, and the C$249 billion 2030 projection. Their talent outlook analysis documents the gap between AI research output and commercial AI employment, the skills mismatches that prevent Canadian businesses from adopting AI tools, and the investment in commercialization programs needed to close the adoption gap. The reference document for anyone trying to understand the actual scale and composition of Canada's digital economy rather than the aspirational version.
