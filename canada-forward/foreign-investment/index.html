Inbound Capital, Competitive Position, and the Restriction Penalty

Foreign Investment in Canada

Canada attracted record foreign investment in 2025 while simultaneously maintaining ownership restrictions that cost the economy C$10 billion annually in foregone productivity. Both of those facts belong in the same analysis.

Research brief · Q2 2026  |  Updated April 2026  |  Canadian Trade Intelligence Inc.
CTI Position

Canada is not struggling to attract investment. It is struggling to attract the right investment consistently and to remove the self-imposed restrictions that prevent productivity-enhancing capital from entering key sectors. The 2025 record is real and should be celebrated. It should not be mistaken for structural progress. The government's correct response is to deepen the non-US investor relationship — Japan, India, the UK, South Korea, Germany — while using the current moment of US uncertainty to make the regulatory reforms on foreign ownership restrictions that successive governments have deferred for thirty years. The window for that reform is now, when the argument that Canada needs diverse capital is politically self-evident rather than technocratically contested. A clear, predictable Investment Canada Act framework for allied-country critical minerals investment is the single highest-leverage action available.

Canada is a top investment destination that keeps finding ways to underperform its own potential

In 2025, foreign direct investment into Canada reached C$96.8 billion, the highest annual inflow since 2007 and the second-best year on record. Canada ranks second in the G20 for FDI stock as a share of GDP, second on the Kearney FDI Confidence Index behind only the United States, and holds the lowest marginal effective tax rate on new business investment in the G7 at 13.0% — far below the American rate of 19.7%. The macroeconomic conditions for attracting capital are, by objective measures, excellent. And yet the dominant narrative in Canadian investment policy circles for the past decade has been one of underperformance, capital flight, and productivity stagnation. Both things are true, and reconciling them is the core task of any serious analysis.

The structural constraint on Canadian FDI is well-documented in the academic literature and largely absent from government communications. Hejazi and Trefler's analysis — the most rigorous available on this question — finds that Canada's restrictive FDI policies in several sectors cost the economy approximately C$10 billion annually in foregone productivity gains. The mechanism is straightforward: foreign-owned firms that enter restricted sectors bring technology, management practices, and global supply chain access that domestically-owned firms in those sectors do not have. When restriction prevents entry, domestic incumbents face less competitive pressure to improve, and productivity stagnates. The sectors most affected are telecommunications, airlines, and financial services — all subject to foreign ownership restrictions that most comparable economies have reduced or eliminated.

The Indo-Pacific dimension of Canadian FDI is both the most important structural opportunity and the most underreported story in this space. British Columbia captured 37% of Canada's Indo-Pacific foreign direct investment in 2024, totalling C$7.1 billion and leading all provinces, driven primarily by critical minerals investments. CEPA with India, which entered into force in April 2026, and CPTPP access create preferential frameworks that should be systematically used to attract Japanese, South Korean, and Indian capital into Canadian processing and manufacturing capacity. The logic for these investors is compelling: clean Canadian electricity for energy-intensive processing, proximity to North American markets via CUSMA, and political stability that their domestic markets cannot always guarantee. The ICA review framework is the binding constraint — not the investment appetite.

Signal Log

Record C$96.8B FDI inflows in 2025 — highest since 2007 FEB 2026

Canada attracted C$96.8 billion in FDI in 2025, driven by M&A activity and the UK emerging as a swing factor at 12% of total inward FDI. Canadian outward investment fell sharply to C$79.4B from C$123B in 2024, reflecting paused Canada-US investment decisions under trade policy uncertainty. The headline record obscures the M&A vs. greenfield composition question that matters most for long-run productivity.

CEPA with India in force — first 12 months the key signal APR 2026

CEPA entered into force in April 2026. The question is whether Indian industrial and financial capital uses it as an entry point to Canadian critical minerals, clean energy, and technology assets. The first 12 months of CEPA-attributable FDI flows will indicate whether the agreement is being used as intended or whether Investment Canada Act uncertainty is suppressing Indian investor interest.

Investment Canada Act critical minerals framework — legibility watch WATCH

Japanese, South Korean, and European institutional investors who want to invest in Canadian critical minerals processing cannot predict with confidence whether a proposed transaction will complete or be blocked. Predictability — not necessarily permissiveness — is the competitive advantage Canada should be cultivating deliberately. Any formal framework publication will be the single most important FDI policy development of 2026.

Foreign ownership restriction review — political window open WATCH

The trade diversification imperative and the defence industrial strategy both create political openings for telecoms and airline foreign ownership reform that have not existed in previous years. Whether any government announces a serious review of these restrictions in 2026 will signal whether the Hejazi-Trefler C$10B productivity cost research has reached policy circles. The window may not stay open.

Key Researchers

Rotman School of Management · University of Toronto
Walid Hejazi and Daniel Trefler

Hejazi and Trefler's work on the productivity consequences of Canada's FDI restrictions is the foundational academic reference for anyone analyzing Canadian investment policy. Their finding that restrictions cost approximately C$10 billion annually in foregone productivity is not a speculative estimate but a carefully constructed empirical result based on industry-level data across decades. Trefler's broader work on trade and productivity has been influential in Canadian trade policy circles for two decades. Hejazi's work on the channels through which FDI affects host country performance — technology transfer, management practice diffusion, and supply chain integration — provides the mechanistic foundation for understanding why FDI openness matters beyond simple capital flows.

researchgate.net →
Fraser Institute
Steven Globerman

Globerman's 2025 Fraser Institute study covering Canadian FDI inflows from 1990 to 2024 provides the most comprehensive longitudinal analysis of Canada's position in global capital markets. His comparison of Canada versus Australia — a structurally similar resource economy that has consistently attracted a larger share of global FDI relative to GDP — is particularly instructive. His conclusion that Canada's absolute FDI numbers can be healthy while its relative competitive position is declining frames the policy question correctly: the issue is not whether Canada is attracting investment but whether it is attracting its fair share given its objective advantages.

fraserinstitute.org →
TD Economics
Canadian FDI Quarterly Analysis

TD Economics produces the most consistently useful real-time analysis of Canadian FDI flows, disaggregating Statistics Canada's balance of payments data to identify sectoral and geographic trends that headline numbers obscure. Their February 2026 analysis identifying the UK as the swing factor in Canada's 2025 record FDI result, and their documentation of the sharp pullback in Canadian outward investment to the United States, provides the current context within which longer-run structural analysis operates.
