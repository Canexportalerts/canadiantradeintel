Canada's energy and cleantech position
Sector context
Canada is a global energy exporter at the edge of a transition
Canada is the world's fourth-largest producer of oil and gas, the second-largest producer of hydroelectricity, and the source of roughly 10% of global uranium supply. LNG Canada Phase 1 (Kitimat) is Canada's first major LNG export terminal, opening a direct Pacific energy export route to Japan, South Korea, and eventually China. The energy transition doesn't diminish Canada's energy role — it multiplies it, adding hydrogen, SMRs, and critical minerals to the export mix.

Key signals this sector
LNG
LNG Canada opens Canada's first Pacific energy export route
LNG Canada Phase 1 (Shell-led, Kitimat, BC) is in final commissioning. First export cargoes to Japan, South Korea, and China signal a structural shift in how Canada sells energy globally. Woodfibre LNG and Cedar LNG (Haisla Nation-led) are next in the queue, with combined capacity to double Canada's LNG export volumes.
Hydrogen
Canada-Germany Hydrogen MoU and Japan framework create export pipelines
Germany's Energiewende creates sustained hydrogen import demand that Canada is positioned to supply via Atlantic ports (Halifax–Rotterdam corridor). Japan's hydrogen import programme offers long-term offtake agreements. Alberta and Quebec are competing to be Canada's primary clean hydrogen export provinces.
Nuclear
SMR development and Cameco uranium position Canada as nuclear exporter
ARC Nuclear, Terrestrial Energy, and Global First Power are advancing Canadian SMR designs for domestic and export deployment. Ontario Power Generation's Darlington SMR (ARC-100) is on track for 2029 grid connection — Canada's first new nuclear build in decades. Cameco's uranium supply contracts make Canada the primary beneficiary of global nuclear revival.
Grid & Storage
Canada's clean grid advantage in the age of AI data centre demand
Canada's electricity grid is 83% non-emitting — among the cleanest in the world. Data centre investment (Microsoft, Google, Amazon) is clustering around Quebec and BC hydro. Grid capacity constraints are the primary choke point. The Clean Electricity Regulation sets 2035 as the target for a fully clean national grid.
