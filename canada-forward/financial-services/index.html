Maple Eight, Banking Stability, and Domestic Capital Repatriation

Financial Services and Pension Capital

Canada manages C$4.5 trillion in pension assets, ranks third globally, and runs the pension model that the World Bank and CalPERS have studied and tried to replicate. The Maple Eight invests 75 cents of every dollar outside Canada. The gap between world-class institutional capacity and domestic investment deployment is the central question of Canadian capital policy.

Research brief · Q2 2026  |  Updated April 2026  |  Canadian Trade Intelligence Inc.
CTI Position

The case for redirecting a portion of Maple Eight investment back to Canada is not an argument for compromising pension fund governance or returns. It is an argument for creating the domestic investable projects and policy frameworks that make Canadian investment competitive with international alternatives. If Ottawa were to incentivize pension funds to double their Canadian stake across real estate, equities, private equity, credit, and infrastructure, the injection would be nearly C$350 billion — the largest single influx of investment capital in Canadian history. That is not a mandatory repatriation policy. It is the consequence of creating infrastructure projects that are well-structured, well-governed, and offering competitive risk-adjusted returns. The government's task is to build the supply of investable Canadian projects. The pension funds will follow the returns.

Canada has the world's most sophisticated pension system and is investing most of it elsewhere

Canada manages C$4.5 trillion in pension assets, placing it third globally by AUM. The Maple Eight together manage approximately C$2.4 trillion. They are fully funded, generate returns that consistently outperform their global peers, and operate under a governance model that the World Bank and California's CalPERS have studied and attempted to replicate. Canada's pension system is genuinely one of the country's most significant economic institutions — a model of institutional investment management that no other country has fully matched. And yet for every dollar managed by the Maple Eight, more than 75 cents are invested outside Canada.

This is not a governance failure. The Maple Eight's mandate is to generate the returns required to fund their beneficiaries' pensions, and they have done that exceptionally well. The global diversification strategy is the correct strategy for maximizing risk-adjusted returns on a C$2.4 trillion portfolio. The question is not whether the pension funds have made the right investment decisions given their mandate. The question is whether Canada's broader policy framework has created the domestic investment opportunities that would make Canadian deployment competitive with international alternatives — and the answer is that it largely has not. Canada's domestic investment opportunity set has been constrained by infrastructure deficits, regulatory complexity, and the general underinvestment in productive capacity that underlies the productivity gap.

The Maple Eight pension capital reallocation cycle is now the single largest potential catalyst for Canadian domestic infrastructure project activation currently visible. CPP Investments and OMERS have publicly flagged increased interest in European infrastructure linked to NATO resilience. The CDPQ model demonstrates that a domestic investment mandate can be implemented without compromising long-run returns — CDPQ's CEO's February 2025 statement that it would finance productivity-boosting projects and help Quebec companies diversify markets in response to US tariff uncertainty signals the potential. The question for federal policy is whether the other Maple Eight funds can be incentivized, without compromising their independence, to adopt a similar domestic investment orientation that aligns with Canada's current diversification and productivity priorities.

Signal Log

Maple Eight reweighting toward defence-adjacent and energy transition assets Q2 2026

Canada's Maple Eight pension funds are actively reweighting allocations toward defence-adjacent infrastructure and domestic energy transition assets — a portfolio shift driven by the repricing of geopolitical risk following NATO's 2025 defence spending commitments. CPP Investments and OMERS have publicly flagged increased interest in European infrastructure linked to NATO resilience. This reallocation cycle is estimated at C$350B if fully redirected domestically.

Airport governance reform enabling pension investment WATCH

The federal government's interest in attracting Canadian pension capital into airport infrastructure requires governance reforms that allow airports to operate as institutional investments rather than non-profit authorities. Canadian airports could become the same as UK, Australian, and New Zealand airports — competitive pension fund infrastructure investments — if the governance and regulatory structure is reformed to make them investable.

NBFI sector leverage — Bank of Canada monitoring WATCH

The Bank of Canada's 2025 Financial Stability Report flags growing leverage in the non-bank financial sector, particularly through repo funding growth among pension funds and hedge funds. The NBFI sector reached 756% of GDP in 2024, up 43% since 2019. Watch for Bank of Canada guidance on NBFI leverage and any OSFI regulatory response to the leverage growth documented in the IMF FSAP.

AIMCo dual mandate discussions WATCH

Reports indicate AIMCo, Alberta's investment manager, is considering a dual mandate similar to CDPQ's. Any formal announcement would establish a second provincial precedent for domestic investment orientation within the Maple Eight model and signal that the political environment for domestic pension repatriation is broadening beyond Quebec.

Key Researchers

Policy Options · IRPP
Pension Fund Domestic Investment Analysis (December 2025)

The December 2025 Policy Options analysis "Why Canada's pension giants should invest more at home" provides the most comprehensive available case for domestic pension capital repatriation. Its calculation that doubling the Maple Eight's Canadian stake would inject nearly C$350 billion into the economy — the largest influx in Canadian history — is based on rigorous analysis of current AUM and domestic allocation data. Its comparison with CDPQ's dual mandate and international precedents for domestic investment requirements provides the policy context. For CTI, this is the essential reference on the intersection of pension fund governance and Canadian industrial strategy.

IMF Financial Sector Assessment Program
Canada FSAP 2025

The IMF's 2025 FSAP for Canada is the most authoritative external assessment of the Canadian financial system's stability, structure, and risks. Its documentation of the banking system's concentration and resilience, the NBFI sector's size and leverage growth, and the pension system's funding status and performance provides the quantitative foundation for any serious analysis of Canadian financial services. The FSAP's finding that Canada's financial institutions reached 756% of GDP in 2024, up 43% since 2019, and the associated leverage monitoring concerns, are the financial stability context that CTI tracks through its weekly reports and terminal data.

Desautels Faculty of Management · McGill University
Sebastien Betermier and CEM Benchmarking

Betermier's research on the Maple Eight model, conducted in partnership with CEM Benchmarking, provides the most rigorous academic analysis of why Canadian pension funds outperform their global peers — 8% average return versus 6% globally, and 60 basis points of value added versus 20 for global peers. His identification of three performance drivers — greater in-house management, redeployment of resources toward value-added strategies, and reallocation of capital — documents the structural advantages of the model and why it has been studied and replicated internationally. Essential context for any policy proposals about domestic investment mandates.
