01

Overview

Brazil is Canada's most important trading partner in South America and the third-largest economy in the Americas, after the United States and Canada. Total bilateral merchandise trade reached C$12.7 billion in 2024 — with Brazilian exports to Canada hitting record levels for the third consecutive year — and two-way services trade adding a further C$1.8 billion. Canadian direct investment in Brazil stands at approximately C$19.8 billion in stock terms, while Brazil's direct investment in Canada at C$15.9 billion makes it Canada's second-largest foreign investor in the Americas after the United States. This is a relationship of substantial and growing commercial weight that remains systematically underappreciated relative to its actual size.

The bilateral trade structure is significantly asymmetric: Brazil exports a diverse range of commodities and manufactured goods to Canada — gold, alumina, aircraft, cane sugar, and increasingly copper and green coffee — while Canada's exports to Brazil are heavily concentrated in potash fertilizers. This concentration creates bilateral export volatility, as illustrated in 2024 when Canada's goods exports to Brazil fell 41.2%, driven almost entirely by a 57.2% decline in potash exports due to fertilizer price normalization. Managing this concentration risk is a defining challenge for Canadian businesses with Brazil market exposure.

Brazil's hosting of COP30 in Belém in November 2025 positioned it as a global focal point for climate and environmental governance, reinforcing President Lula's green growth agenda and creating forward-looking context for Canada-Brazil collaboration on critical minerals, clean energy technology, and sustainable supply chains. The bilateral Canada-Brazil Defence Cooperation Agreement entering into force in February 2025 adds a new dimension to a relationship previously anchored primarily in trade and investment.

President Lula's third term, beginning January 2023, has been characterized by a return to Brazilian internationalism after the Bolsonaro government's more inward-looking posture. Lula has actively rebuilt multilateral engagement, hosted the G20 presidency in 2024, and positioned Brazil as a leader in global climate governance through COP30. His government's "ecological transformation" agenda — framing environmental protection and economic development as compatible — creates a receptive policy environment for Canadian cleantech, critical minerals, and sustainable supply chain investment.

Domestically, the Lula government operates within Brazil's complex congressional coalition dynamics. Fiscal pressures and the Brazilian real's significant depreciation in 2024 — which directly reduced Canadian export volumes by making Canadian goods more expensive in local currency — are structural challenges that define the operational context for Canadian businesses in the Brazilian market. Despite these pressures, Brazil's institutional framework: an independent central bank, sophisticated financial sector, and functioning democratic institutions provides the structural stability long-term commercial relationships require.

Brazil surprised to the upside in 2024 with GDP growth of approximately 3.4%, driven by strong agricultural performance, a resilient labour market at historic low unemployment, and robust consumer spending supported by cash transfer programmes. The agribusiness sector — Brazil is the world's largest exporter of soybeans, sugar, coffee, beef, and orange juice — delivered another exceptional harvest year, anchoring export earnings and moderating current account pressure despite the real's depreciation against major currencies.

Brazil's long-term structural position is compelling: the country holds the world's largest reserves of niobium (90%+ of global production), significant deposits of rare earth elements, lithium, copper, iron ore, and manganese. The Banco Central's Selic rate elevated to approximately 15% in 2025 reflects persistent inflation above target, creating a challenging domestic financing environment while attracting short-term capital flows. The BRL depreciation in 2024 is the single most important operational variable for Canadian businesses — it directly suppressed Canadian export revenues to Brazil and will continue to affect bilateral trade volumes until currency stabilization occurs.

Top Canadian exports to Brazil: Potash and fertilizer products have historically dominated Canadian exports to Brazil, accounting for nearly half of total goods export value — Brazil is among the world's largest fertilizer importers, and Canadian potash from Saskatchewan's Nutrien and Mosaic operations feeds directly into Brazil's massive soybean and corn production complex. Beyond potash, Canada exports aerospace components and aircraft, non-electric engines and industrial machinery, and pharmaceutical products. The 2024 fertilizer price normalization exposed the concentration risk structurally embedded in this export profile.

Top Canadian imports from Brazil: Gold is the largest single product by value (approximately 28% of Brazilian exports to Canada in recent periods), reflecting Canadian gold refining demand and Brazil's significant gold production. Calcined alumina — used as feedstock for Canadian aluminum smelters — is a structurally important bilateral commodity flow. Embraer aircraft and aerospace equipment, cane sugar and ethanol, and increasingly copper, green coffee, and dielectric transformers (up 359% in 2024) round out the import profile. The fastest-growing import categories reflect a broadening relationship beyond traditional commodities.

05

Market Access

WTO MFN
No Preferential FTA — MFN Tariffs Apply
Canada and Brazil do not have a bilateral or regional free trade agreement in force. Trade operates under WTO Most Favoured Nation tariff schedules, meaning Canadian exporters face Brazil's applied MFN tariff rates — historically elevated for manufactured goods and some agricultural products — without the preferential access available in Canada's CETA or CPTPP markets. Canada-Mercosur FTA negotiations have been ongoing at a measured pace and represent the most likely future framework for preferential access.
Trade Agreements guide — Canada coverage

Brazil's regulatory and market access environment has historically been complex: high average tariffs for non-preferential partners, a layered domestic tax system (ICMS, IPI, PIS/COFINS) that creates effective market access barriers beyond headline tariff rates, and import licensing requirements in specific sectors. The Lula government's economic reform agenda — including a major VAT/tax simplification reform approved in 2023 — is gradually reducing this complexity, but Brazilian regulatory compliance remains more demanding than comparable OECD markets. TCS offices in Brasília, São Paulo, and Rio de Janeiro provide navigation support.

⚠ Monitor
Canada-Mercosur FTA negotiations remain the key bilateral market access development to track. A completed agreement would meaningfully alter the bilateral trade framework — providing preferential tariff access for Canadian goods into Brazil and formal government procurement access. No completion timeline is established as of Q1 2026.
06

Opportunity Assessment

Critical Minerals Co-investment · STRONG
Brazil holds the world's largest niobium reserves (90%+ of global production at CBMM in Araxá), significant rare earth deposits, lithium in Minas Gerais, copper in Pará, and the world's fourth-largest iron ore reserves. Canadian mining companies have both investment and technology transfer opportunities in Brazil's critical minerals sector as it transitions from extraction to value-added processing.
Agri-food Technology & Inputs · STRONG
Brazil's agricultural productivity machine requires world-class inputs: precision agriculture technology, crop protection, genetics, and agronomic services. Canadian agri-tech companies in precision agriculture, biological crop protection, drones, and farm management software have identifiable market openings. The potash trade relationship creates distribution and relationship infrastructure for broader Canadian agri-input companies.
Cleantech & Energy Transition · STRONG
Brazil's energy matrix is already heavily renewable (over 80% hydroelectric), and Lula's COP30 leadership creates political momentum for further green investment. Canadian cleantech companies in grid management, energy storage, sustainable fuels, and environmental services have partnership opportunities. The bilateral green growth dialogue accelerated through COP30 creates government-to-government channels benefiting Canadian companies.
Financial Services & Investment · STRONG
Canadian pension funds have significant Brazil exposure. CPP Investments has historically held Brazilian infrastructure and private equity positions. Brazil's deep capital markets (B3 is one of the world's largest stock exchanges), large insurance market, and growing fintech ecosystem create opportunities for Canadian financial services firms with Latin America strategies.
Aerospace & Defence · EMERGING
The 2025 Canada-Brazil Defence Cooperation Agreement formalizes a bilateral defence industrial relationship. Canadian defence companies with capabilities in cybersecurity, surveillance, and aerospace maintenance have Brazil market opportunities given Embraer's global supply chain integration and Brazil's own defence modernization programme.
Importers: Brazilian Goods into Canada · VERY ACTIVE
Gold, alumina, Embraer aircraft, beef, orange juice, coffee, and sugar are significant Canadian import categories sourced from Brazil. Brazilian export volumes to Canada hit records in 2024 (up 9.44% YoY). Canadian importers should audit their Brazilian supply chain exposure for BRL currency risk and deforestation due diligence requirements under emerging EU and Canadian ESG frameworks.
07

Canadian Business Presence

Canadian mining companies constitute the largest Canadian business presence in Brazil by investment value. Kinross Gold, Equinox Gold, and others have producing and exploration assets in Brazil's Minas Gerais, Pará, and Mato Grosso states. The Brazil-Canada Chamber of Commerce (CCBC) estimates Canadian direct investment in Brazil at approximately C$19.8 billion, concentrated in mining, financial services, and agri-business.

Nutrien and Mosaic — the dominant Canadian potash producers — are the most commercially impactful Canadian business presences in Brazil even without local operations, given Brazil's status as one of the world's largest potash importers. The fertilizer trade relationship is the single most operationally significant bilateral commodity link. When potash prices move, Canadian export revenues to Brazil move in direct proportion — as illustrated dramatically in 2024 when exports fell 41.2%.

Embraer's relationship with Canadian aviation is bidirectional: Embraer's E-series regional jets operate in Canadian regional fleets, and the company has Canadian-origin components in its supply chains. CAE, Canada's flight simulation leader, has Brazilian civil and defence training market operations. The Canada-Brazil bilateral relationship is supported by the Brazil-Canada Chamber of Commerce (CCBC), which organizes approximately eight annual trade missions from Brazil to Canada covering AI, food, mining, healthcare, and industrial sectors. TCS offices in Brasília, São Paulo, and Rio de Janeiro provide active Canadian business support.

Risk Register

Risk CategoryLevelAssessment
PoliticalModerateLula's PT-led coalition requires complex congressional management; fiscal pressures constrain policy delivery. Brazilian democratic institutions are functioning but polarization remains elevated following the 2022 election. No foreseeable disruption to bilateral commercial framework. The 2025 Defence Cooperation Agreement and strategic partnership infrastructure provide institutional insulation from political cycles.
CurrencyElevatedThe Brazilian real (BRL) is subject to significant volatility driven by commodity prices, US dollar strength, domestic fiscal dynamics, and risk appetite for emerging market currencies. The 2024 BRL depreciation directly caused a 41.2% decline in Canadian goods exports to Brazil. Canadian exporters with BRL-denominated contracts or Brazilian importers purchasing CAD-priced goods face meaningful currency exposure. Hedging is essential for contracts exceeding 3–6 months.
RegulatoryModerateBrazil's regulatory environment is complex: layered tax system, import licensing requirements, local content rules in certain sectors, and bureaucratic approval processes. The 2023 tax reform simplifies VAT but implementation is phased over several years. Canadian companies entering Brazil benefit significantly from local legal and regulatory advisory support. TCS Brazil offices provide navigation assistance.
CommercialModerateBrazilian commercial counterparties range from world-class multinationals (Vale, Embraer, JBS) to SMEs with variable creditworthiness. Contract enforcement through Brazilian courts is functional but slow. EDC provides political risk insurance and financing support for Canadian exporters managing Brazil market risk. Credit assessment of Brazilian buyers is advisable for new commercial relationships.
Environmental / ESGModerateAmazon deforestation and biodiversity commitments are central to Brazil's COP30 positioning and to EU CBAM and supply chain due diligence regulations. Canadian companies sourcing Brazilian agri-food, beef, soy, or timber face increasing scrutiny on deforestation exposure. The Lula government's Amazon protection agenda reduces this risk compared to the Bolsonaro period but does not eliminate it.
Supply ChainModerateBrazil's logistics infrastructure is constrained relative to its economic scale: port congestion at Santos and Paranaguá during harvest seasons and inadequate road infrastructure in agricultural interior regions add cost and time variability. Brazilian government infrastructure investment programmes are addressing these constraints but at a pace slower than demand growth.
09

Procurement Pipeline

Without an FTA in force, Canadian companies do not have formal preferential access to Brazilian government procurement. Brazil has not acceded to the WTO Government Procurement Agreement, meaning Canadian companies access Brazilian public procurement on a non-preferential basis, competing with domestic suppliers who benefit from local content preferences in many sectors. This is a meaningful barrier compared to CETA or CPTPP markets.

🔗
Compras.gov.br — Federal Procurement Portal
Brazil's federal government procurement portal publishes tenders from federal agencies and state-owned enterprises. International companies can participate in open international tenders, particularly in infrastructure, ICT, energy, and research equipment. Access at gov.br/compras. Portal is in Portuguese; local representation is advisable for formal bid submissions.
🔗
Petrobras Supply Chain — Pre-salt Investment Programme
Petrobras's ongoing pre-salt deepwater oil and gas investment programme — one of the world's largest upstream capital programmes — generates sustained procurement in subsea equipment, drilling technology, engineering services, and environmental monitoring. Canadian oil and gas equipment and services companies have supply chain participation opportunities. TCS Rio de Janeiro provides introduction support.
🔗
BNDES — Critical Minerals & Cleantech Finance · 2024–2030
Brazil's national development bank (BNDES) is deploying substantial capital into critical minerals processing, clean energy, and green hydrogen infrastructure under the Lula government's ecological transformation agenda. BNDES-financed projects create procurement and partnership opportunities for Canadian cleantech and mining technology companies. EDC has an active co-financing relationship with BNDES on select Latin American development projects.
🔗
TCS Brasília, São Paulo & Rio de Janeiro — CanExport Support
Three TCS offices in Brazil provide Canadian companies with procurement pipeline intelligence, partner introductions, and navigational support through Brazil's complex regulatory environment. CanExport SME funding is eligible for Brazil market development. The Brazil-Canada Chamber of Commerce (CCBC) complements TCS with trade mission and business matching programmes.
10

Government Signals

📄
Canada-Brazil Defence Cooperation Agreement · February 2025
The Canada-Brazil Defence Cooperation Agreement entered into force in February 2025, followed by Political-Military talks in May 2025. The agreement formalizes a bilateral defence industrial relationship and creates mechanisms for cooperation in defence technology, cybersecurity, and peacekeeping — adding a new dimension to a relationship previously anchored in trade and investment.
📄
Canada-Brazil Strategic Partnership Dialogue · Ongoing
The bilateral Foreign Minister-led Strategic Partnership Dialogue — with its fourth edition held in 2023 in Brasília — provides the highest-level bilateral mechanism for advancing commercial, environmental, and security cooperation. The dialogue's focus on critical minerals, clean energy, and democratic governance alignment creates a government-to-government framework that Canadian companies operating in Brazil can reference in commercial development.
📄
COP30 — Belém, Brazil · November 2025
Brazil hosted COP30 in Belém in November 2025, with Canada an active participant. Canada and Brazil are committed to environmental sustainability and green growth as explicit bilateral priorities. The COP30 context accelerated bilateral green investment and cleantech cooperation discussions, and the momentum is expected to generate concrete partnership announcements through 2026.
📄
Canada-Mercosur FTA Negotiations — Ongoing
Canada and Mercosur (Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay) have been engaged in FTA negotiations for several years, with periodic rounds of talks. A completed Canada-Mercosur FTA would significantly alter the bilateral trade framework — providing preferential tariff access for Canadian goods into Brazil and formal government procurement access. No completion timeline is established as of Q1 2026; progress has been incremental.
