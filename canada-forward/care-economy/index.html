Childcare, Elder Care, and the Workforce That Makes Everything Else Possible

Care Economy

Canada's largest sector is also its most invisible. Paid care jobs generate at least 13% of GDP and 22% of all employment. Unpaid care work contributes an estimated C$860 billion annually — roughly 37% of GDP. Canada has approximately five to eight years to build elder care capacity before the baby boom reaches its highest-need years.

Research brief · Q2 2026  |  Updated April 2026  |  Canadian Trade Intelligence Inc.
CTI Position

The political economy of care is slowly shifting in Canada. The C$10-per-day childcare system has demonstrated that care investment generates measurable economic returns — C$32 billion in additional GDP in 2024. The next step is elder care: applying the same infrastructure-investment logic to long-term care and home care capacity that was applied to childcare. The demographic window for building that capacity before it is urgently needed is narrow. Canada has approximately five to eight years to substantially expand its elder care workforce, home care infrastructure, and long-term care capacity before the leading edge of the baby boom cohort reaches its highest-need years. A government that treats this as a social expenditure will be unprepared. A government that treats it as infrastructure investment — with the same economic analysis applied to transit or housing — will build something that serves the country for a generation.

Canada's largest sector is also its most invisible

Care work — childcare, elder care, disability support, healthcare, and the unpaid domestic labour that underpins all of it — is the foundation on which the rest of Canada's economy operates. The Canadian Labour Congress estimates that paid care jobs generate at least 13% of Canada's GDP and account for 22% of all jobs. Unpaid care work is estimated at up to C$860 billion annually, or roughly 37% of GDP. That exceeds the combined contribution of manufacturing, wholesale trade, and retail. These numbers are not disputed in the academic literature. They are simply not incorporated into the economic analysis that drives policy and investment decisions.

The demographic pressure on Canada's care systems is structural and accelerating. Canada's 65+ population now represents 17% of the total and is projected to reach 23% by 2031. The demand for elder care, home care, long-term care, and associated healthcare services will grow faster than the overall economy for at least the next two decades regardless of policy choices. This is not a forecast. It is the mechanical consequence of age cohort demographics established decades ago. Every year that passes without significant expansion of Canada's care infrastructure and care workforce creates a larger gap between what the aging population will need and what the system will be able to provide.

The Canada-wide Early Learning and Child Care system, launched with C$30 billion in federal funding from 2021-22 to 2025-26, represents the most significant federal investment in care infrastructure in Canadian history. The Centre for Future Work estimates that C$32 billion in additional GDP was generated in 2024 from expanded ELCC — through direct sector output, supply chain effects, and increased female labour force participation — and that the expansion likely prevented Canada from experiencing a technical recession in the second half of 2023. This is not a social program that costs the economy. It is an investment in productive capacity that pays back through labour force expansion and productivity gains. The elder care equivalent of this analysis has not yet driven federal policy at scale. That is the policy gap.

Signal Log

$10/day childcare — 8 provinces operational Q1 2026

Eight of ten provinces are operating regulated childcare below $10/day as of Q1 2026. PEI and Nova Scotia met the full target in 2025. Ontario, BC, and Quebec are phasing toward full fee reductions through 2026. Alberta remains in negotiation. The economic returns — C$32B additional GDP in 2024 — provide the strongest available evidence for care as infrastructure investment.

Immigration cuts — care sector staffing gaps emerging Q1 2026

The federal decision to cut temporary residents to below 5% of population by 2027 is affecting care sector hiring. Home care, long-term care, and ECE are flagging staffing shortfalls in Q1 2026. Healthcare employers filed a joint brief to IRCC requesting sector-specific TFW carve-outs in March 2026. The intersection of immigration policy and care workforce supply is a structural tension with no easy resolution.

Long-term care national standards — final Q3 2026 WATCH

Federal consultation on national LTC standards closed February 2026 with over 3,000 submissions. Health Canada expects final standards in Q3 2026, setting minimum staff-to-resident ratios and inspection regimes. This is the most significant federal intervention in long-term care governance since the COVID-19 pandemic exposed the sector's structural inadequacy.

Canada Disability Benefit — first payments Q2 2026 NOV 2025

The November 2025 budget included $1.3B over three years for the Canada Disability Benefit, with first payments confirmed for Q2 2026. This is both a care economy investment and a labour force measure — ensuring that people with disabilities have the income support that enables economic participation and reduces unpaid care burden on families.

Key Researchers

Canadian Labour Congress
Care Economy Research and Advocacy

The CLC's March 2026 brief on the care economy as Canada's untapped economic superpower synthesizes the most comprehensive available picture of care's contribution to GDP, its workforce demographics, and the policy case for a Care Economy Commission. Their framing of the economic value of unpaid care — estimated at C$860 billion or 37% of GDP — is the most ambitious and well-documented quantification available. Their advocacy for a coordinated federal strategy brings together unions and women's rights organizations in a policy coalition whose research foundation is solid. Essential reading for understanding the full economic scale of what Canadian care policy is governing.

Centre for Future Work
ELCC Economic Impact Analysis

The Centre for Future Work's November 2024 analysis of the C$10-per-day childcare plan's economic effects is the most rigorous available assessment of the ELCC system's macroeconomic returns. Their C$32 billion GDP estimate for 2024 uses a multi-channel framework accounting for direct ELCC sector output, supply chain spending, and the labour force participation effects of reduced childcare costs. Their documentation that expanded ELCC may have prevented a 2023 technical recession provides the strongest available economic evidence for childcare as infrastructure investment rather than social expenditure.

centreforfuturework.ca →
Statistics Canada and Academic Researchers
Care Economy Valuation Research

Statistics Canada's 2023 Conceptual Framework for the Canadian Care Economy and the associated academic research by Springer Nature on family caregiver valuation represent the most methodologically rigorous available approaches to measuring care's economic contribution. The replacement cost estimate of C$97.1 to C$112.7 billion for unpaid care annually, based on 5.7 billion hours of care work, provides a lower-bound measure of the care economy's hidden GDP contribution. Their finding that living arrangement explains 81-83% of the variance in unpaid care contributions has direct implications for housing and urban design policy.
