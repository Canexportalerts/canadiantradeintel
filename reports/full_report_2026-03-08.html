Price Intelligence

At $58/kg, rare earth prices remain 18.8% below last year despite Chinese export controls, creating a window for Canadian producers to lock long-term contracts while prices stabilize. Western buyers are paying 20-40% premiums over Chinese spot prices for non-Chinese supply, directly benefiting Canada's Vital Metals and future production from Appia's Alces Lake project.

→ Canada's rare earth sector sits in a unique position as the only non-Chinese integrated mine-to-magnet supply chain, with US defense buyers actively seeking Canadian supply to meet 2027 de-risking mandates.

Demand Geography

The US Department of Defense is actively funding Canadian rare earth projects under the mandate to eliminate Chinese dependence by 2027, requiring ~5,000 tonnes of neodymium-praseodymium oxide annually. Japan's JOGMEC is increasing investment in Canadian projects following the 2010 China embargo lesson, targeting 3,000 tonnes annually through the Canada-Japan Critical Minerals Partnership. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act designates Canada as a strategic partner to meet their 40% non-Chinese processing target by 2030, representing 4,000 tonnes of annual demand.

Supply Gap Analysis

China controls 85-90% of global rare earth processing while simultaneously tightening export controls on critical elements like dysprosium and neodymium since April 2025. Only two commercial-scale separation facilities exist outside China - one in the US and Canada's nascent SRC Saskatoon facility - creating severe bottlenecks for EV motor magnet production growing at 25% annually.

🇨🇦 Canada's Competitive Position

Canada is the only non-Chinese country with both operational mining (Vital Metals' Nechalacho) and processing capacity (SRC Saskatoon facility producing neodymium, praseodymium, and samarium). The entire Canadian rare earth supply chain operates without Chinese ownership, qualifying for US defense procurement under CUSMA and making Canadian supply uniquely attractive to Western buyers seeking supply chain security.

⚠ Risk Flags

Chinese export control escalation could disrupt global pricing mechanisms and create sudden supply shocks that Canadian producers must be prepared to fulfill on short notice. Companies must ensure processing capabilities can scale rapidly to meet strategic buyer requirements when supply disruptions occur.
