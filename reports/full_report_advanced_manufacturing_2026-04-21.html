Intelligence Overview

President Trump's April 6 expansion of Section 232 tariffs to full product value—eliminating Canada's previous exemptions—immediately raises steel and aluminum input costs for Canadian manufacturers by up to 50%, forcing urgent supply chain recalibration and pricing adjustments. Advanced manufacturers dependent on US-sourced steel and aluminum must accelerate nearshoring strategies to Mexico or Mexico-Canada partnerships, or lock in long-term domestic sourcing contracts now. Watch the Spring 2026 Defence Investment Agency legislation enabling $180 billion in Canadian procurement opportunities; defence contractors can offset tariff impacts by capturing protected domestic contracts before competition intensifies.

As of mid-April 2026, the Buy Canadian policy has applied to solicitations valued at around $3.6 billion with $527.9 million worth of contracts awarded to date. The policy prioritizes Canadian content, materials, suppliers, and workers in federal procurement of $37 billion annually. For exporters, this demonstrates Canada's commitment to domestic supply chains which could influence other countries' procurement policies.

· Reshoring Immediate
US manufacturers are accelerating nearshoring to Canada and Mexico amid 50% steel and aluminum tariffs, with companies r
A new US policy reduces steel and aluminum tariffs to 25% for Canada and Mexico-based companies that supply US auto manufacturers and commit to constructing or expanding primary steel or aluminum production facilities in the US. This policy signals strong US emphasis on nearshoring and reshoring manufacturing for essential raw materials. The tariff adjustment stems from Trump administration's April 2026 proclamation imposing 50% tariffs on certain aluminum and steel goods.
Risk AssessmentCanadian steel and aluminum producers face significant pressure from 50% US tariffs but can access reduced 25% rates by committing to US facility investments. This creates opportunities for Canadian companies to expand into the US market through strategic partnerships and facility development. However, it also risks shifting Canadian production capacity to the US, potentially reducing domestic manufacturing employment and capacity.

· Steel Aluminum Input Costs Immediate
US implemented major Section 232 tariff restructuring on April 6, 2026, shifting from metal-content-only to full-value t
Trump administration's April 2 proclamation raised tariffs to 50% on Canadian steel/aluminum and expanded scope to include copper. New tariffs apply to full product value instead of just metal content, dramatically increasing costs for Canadian manufacturers exporting derivative products. Commerce Department also established adjustment program allowing 25% reduced tariffs for Canadian producers who commit to US facility expansion.
Risk AssessmentCanadian manufacturers face significantly higher input costs for US-origin metals and derivative products, while export opportunities may emerge for companies willing to invest in US facilities under the new adjustment program. Auto parts makers like Linamar and Martinrea will see increased costs on some products but most automotive goods remain exempt under separate agreements.
