No trade relationship in the world matches the depth of integration between Canada and the United States. $3.5 billion in goods crosses the shared border every day. Canada is the largest export market for 36 American states. The relationship is not simply commercial — it is structural. Canadian and American supply chains are so deeply intertwined in automotive, aerospace, energy, and agriculture that "Canadian" and "American" products often contain components from both countries multiple times before reaching an end customer.

The surplus Canada runs with the US — $170B in 2024 — is largely driven by energy exports ($176B in Canadian energy products to the US in 2024). Stripping out energy, the goods relationship is significantly more balanced. This matters because US political framing of the "trade deficit" often ignores energy, creating a distorted picture that has been used to justify tariff actions.

Key Canadian exports: Energy products (crude oil, natural gas, electricity), motor vehicles and parts, machinery, precious metals, lumber and forest products, agri-food.

Key Canadian imports from the US: Machinery and equipment, motor vehicles and parts, electronic equipment, plastics and chemicals, pharmaceutical products.

⚑ WATCH
The 25% tariff on non-CUSMA-compliant goods announced in early 2025 and the 10% energy tariff represent the most significant disruption to Canada–US trade since NAFTA. Canadian exports to the US fell 15.7% in April 2025 before partially recovering. The 2026 CUSMA joint review is the defining trade policy event on Canada's horizon. See full CUSMA analysis →
02

Market Opportunity

The US market is simultaneously Canada's greatest opportunity and its most familiar — which means it is also the most underexplored. Canadian businesses that have saturated their domestic market have an immediate, tariff-advantaged export market of 335 million consumers next door. The strategic question is not whether to be in the US market, but how deeply and in which segments.

Critical Minerals & Clean Energy
US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) provisions treat Canadian critical minerals as domestic inputs for EV manufacturing credits. Canadian lithium, cobalt, nickel, and graphite producers have a structural preference in the US supply chain that competitors from non-FTA countries cannot match. This is a multi-decade opportunity.
Technology & Digital Services
Canadian tech companies — particularly in AI, fintech, and cybersecurity — have natural US market entry via shared language, legal framework, and CUSMA's digital trade provisions. The US is the primary scale market for Canadian tech unicorns and scale-ups. CUSMA prohibits data localization requirements, protecting cross-border data flows.
Advanced Manufacturing
CUSMA's rules of origin (75% North American content for autos) incentivize US manufacturers to source Canadian inputs. Nearshoring trends accelerated by US-China tensions are redirecting supply chain investment toward Canada and Mexico. Canadian manufacturers with US-compatible quality standards have significant procurement access.
Professional Services
CUSMA's temporary entry provisions allow Canadian professionals — engineers, accountants, lawyers, consultants, scientists — to work in the US more easily than nationals of almost any other country. The TN visa category is efficient and broad. Canadian professional services firms have effectively a domestic-equivalent market across the border.
Agri-food
Despite CUSMA's dairy concessions, Canadian agri-food exports to the US are substantial and growing in premium and organic categories. Canadian canola, beef, pork, and seafood all have significant US market presence. Geographic proximity minimizes cold chain costs that disadvantage more distant suppliers.
Government Procurement
WTO-AGP membership gives Canadian suppliers access to US federal procurement above threshold values. Canadian defence and aerospace companies have deep US procurement relationships — Pratt & Whitney Canada, CAE, and L3 Technologies Canada all have major US government contracts. Buy American provisions complicate but do not eliminate access.
03

Investment Climate

The US is the largest single destination for Canadian foreign direct investment globally — every major Canadian company has US operations, and most Canadian businesses with growth ambitions are effectively planning US market entry as an early strategic step. The investment relationship flows in both directions: the US is also Canada's largest source of inbound foreign investment.

The investment climate has become more politically complex under recent US administrations. Subsidies competition — driven by the IRA and CHIPS Act — has created tension as the US offers incentives that Canadian jurisdictions struggle to match. The US has used national security review (CFIUS) more aggressively to scrutinize foreign investment in sensitive sectors, though Canadian investors have not been primary targets.

Major Canadian investors in the US: Brookfield Asset Management (infrastructure, real estate, renewable energy), all Big Five Canadian banks (US retail and commercial banking), Manulife and Sun Life (insurance and wealth management), Thomson Reuters, Lululemon, Shopify US operations, TC Energy and Enbridge (pipeline infrastructure), and hundreds of smaller Canadian companies.

04

Risk Profile

The paradox of the Canada–US relationship is that its depth is simultaneously its greatest strength and its greatest risk. A relationship that accounts for 76% of Canadian exports creates massive efficiency — and massive concentration risk. Any disruption to Canada–US trade has immediate, economy-wide consequences for Canada that equivalent disruptions to any other bilateral relationship would not.

05

Trade Agreement Coverage

CUSMA⚑ Under Review — 2026
Canada–United States–Mexico Agreement. Replaced NAFTA July 1, 2020. Governs the $924B Canada–US goods relationship plus services, digital trade, investment, intellectual property, and labour standards. Includes a mandatory 6-year joint review cycle — the first review is scheduled for 2026.
Key practical provisions for Canadian businesses: Self-certified certificates of origin (no government form required). TN visa category for 63 professional occupations. Digital trade chapter prohibiting data localization. Enhanced IP protections. 75% North American content threshold for automotive tariff preference.
The 2026 review is the most consequential trade policy event on Canada's near-term horizon. The US has used review periods as negotiating leverage historically. Outcomes are uncertain but could affect every major Canadian export sector.

06

Canadian Government Signals

📄
Canada's Trade Diversification Strategy · 2018–ongoing
The federal government has maintained an explicit trade diversification strategy since 2018, aimed at reducing dependence on the US market. CETA, CPTPP, and ongoing FTA negotiations are all instruments of this strategy. The 2025 tariff escalation significantly accelerated this agenda.
📄
CUSMA Implementation Office · Global Affairs Canada · Ongoing
Global Affairs Canada maintains a dedicated CUSMA implementation office that publishes guidance for Canadian businesses on rules of origin, dispute resolution, and chapter-specific provisions. This is the primary resource for navigating the agreement operationally.
📄
Team Canada Trade Mission Response · 2025
Following the 2025 US tariff escalation, the federal government launched coordinated Team Canada trade missions to diversification markets — EU, Indo-Pacific, and Latin America — signalling a structural shift in Canadian trade strategy away from singular US dependence. EDC and BDC expanded diversification financing instruments simultaneously.
07

Procurement Pipeline

US federal procurement is the world's largest government procurement market — estimated at over $700B USD annually. Canadian suppliers have access under the WTO-AGP and CUSMA provisions, though Buy American requirements in certain programs create friction.

Where Canadian businesses have strongest access: Defence and aerospace (long-standing NORAD and NATO supply chain integration — Canadian defence companies are deeply embedded in US procurement), professional services and IT consulting (TN visa status simplifies personnel deployment), energy infrastructure (CUSMA provisions support cross-border pipeline and grid investment), and environmental services.

Where access is more restricted: Infrastructure construction under Buy America provisions (federal infrastructure funding often requires US-made materials), some defence manufacturing under Buy American Act protections, and state-level procurement which is governed separately from federal rules.

The US CHIPS Act and IRA have created new procurement and subsidy opportunities for Canadian companies with US operations or US-eligible supply chains.
