What Happened This Week

BMI, the research unit of Fitch Group, reported this week that global shipments of Chinese export-controlled rare earths - including neodymium, dysprosium, and terbium used in permanent magnets for EV motors and defence equipment - have remained well below historical levels despite a summit between US President Trump and Chinese President Xi. No diplomatic breakthrough loosened China's export controls, which have been active since April 2025.

Intelligence Overview

Mangrove Lithium opened North America's first commercial electrochemical lithium refinery in Delta, BC on April 16, 2026, producing battery-grade lithium hydroxide at scale for the first time domestically. This facility eliminates Canadian dependence on foreign refining for EV battery supply chains and positions BC-based manufacturers as competitive suppliers to North American automakers. Watch India-Canada CEPA negotiations concluding this quarter, which could secure preferential access to Indian critical minerals and accelerate joint cleantech manufacturing agreements.

What We Think
— CTI Editorial Desk  ·  June 02, 2026

This week marks a watershed moment for Canadian critical minerals exports with India and Indonesia emerging as transformed opportunities through new trade agreements and financing commitments. The $2.6 billion uranium deal with India and Export Development Canada's $825 million Indonesia commitment signal a strategic pivot toward Asian markets that could define the sector for years. While established markets like the United States and Europe remain stable with strong fundamentals, the real action is in emerging partnerships where Canadian producers can establish first-mover advantages before competitors catch up. The G7 Critical Minerals Alliance's $18.5 billion mobilization provides the financing framework for Canadian companies to capitalize on these opportunities, but speed matters – windows for preferred supplier status close quickly once established players enter new markets.
