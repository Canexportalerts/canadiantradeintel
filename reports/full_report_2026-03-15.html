$68.50
→0.0% WoW   ▲11.9% YoY
RECOVERY
Price Intelligence

At $68.50/lb, uranium remains below the $75-80/lb that utilities are paying for long-term contracts, creating immediate arbitrage opportunities for Canadian producers with spot market flexibility. The 11.9% year-over-year price increase reflects tightening supply, but Canadian producers should prioritize contract sales over spot given the $6.50-11.50/lb premium utilities are paying for supply security.

→ Canadian uranium producers should prioritize long-term contracts over spot sales to capture the $6.50-11.50/lb premium utilities are paying, with India market entry through the March 2026 Canada-India Critical Minerals Dialogue representing the highest-value immediate opportunity.

Demand Geography

India represents the most active buying opportunity, with NPCIL requiring 2,000 tonnes U3O8/year by 2030 to fuel 22 operating PHWRs and 10 reactors under construction toward their 100 GW nuclear target by 2047. China continues as the largest global buyer at 25,000 tonnes/year by 2030, with recent diplomatic improvements creating re-engagement possibilities for Canadian suppliers. The UK's approval of 8 new large reactors and South Korea's nuclear expansion reversal both create immediate procurement needs from utilities actively seeking non-Russian supply alternatives.

Supply Gap Analysis

A structural 20 million pound annual uranium deficit is expected by 2030, driven by Kazakhstan infrastructure constraints, the US ban on Russian uranium imports, and Niger's 4,500 tonne production gap following political instability. Canada's Athabasca Basin deposits average 20 times world-average grades, with Cameco alone controlling 15% of global primary supply, positioning Canadian producers to capture premium pricing as Western utilities prioritize supply security over cost.

🇨🇦 Canada's Competitive Position

Canada uniquely combines the world's highest-grade uranium deposits in the Athabasca Basin with established processing infrastructure in Saskatchewan and zero Chinese state ownership in the supply chain. The country's CANDU reactor technology creates natural demand pull particularly in India's 22 PHWR fleet, while existing nuclear cooperation agreements with all major importing nations eliminate regulatory barriers that constrain other suppliers.

⚠ Risk Flags

No immediate compliance risks this week, though Canadian exporters should monitor potential changes to nuclear cooperation agreements as geopolitical tensions evolve. Long-term contract negotiations should include force majeure clauses covering transportation disruptions given ongoing global supply chain vulnerabilities.

Price Intelligence

At $24,800/tonne, cobalt remains 8.5% below year-ago levels but sits near a 5-year low, creating entry opportunities for Canadian producers with cost-competitive byproduct operations. The consensus forecast of $35,000-40,000/tonne by 2027 represents 40-60% upside driven by EU Battery Regulation compliance demand that directly favors Canadian supply over DRC alternatives.

→ Canadian cobalt is positioned for significant price premiums and market share gains as EU Battery Regulation compliance creates structural demand for conflict-free alternatives to DRC supply starting in 2025.

Demand Geography

South Korea leads active buying with Samsung SDI, SK Innovation, and LG Energy Solution requiring 15,000 tonnes annually of non-DRC cobalt to comply with EU Battery Regulation for their European exports. The European Union represents the largest opportunity at 25,000 tonnes by 2027, driven by mandatory supply chain traceability requirements starting 2025 that effectively exclude DRC sources. Japan's Panasonic and Sumitomo are expanding battery production with 8,000 tonnes annual demand, supported by the 2023 Canada-Japan Critical Minerals Partnership.

Supply Gap Analysis

The DRC controls 70% of global cobalt supply but faces increasing exclusion from Western supply chains due to OFAC designation risk and artisanal mining ESG violations under EU Battery Regulation compliance requirements. Canadian producers Vale and Glencore in Sudbury are uniquely positioned to fill this 48,000+ tonne annual gap with conflict-free, byproduct cobalt that meets all regulatory requirements without additional compliance costs.

🇨🇦 Canada's Competitive Position

The Sudbury basin is the largest cobalt-producing region outside the DRC, with Vale and Glencore operating cost-competitive byproduct operations that maintain existing relationships with all major battery manufacturers. Canadian cobalt carries zero conflict mineral exposure and is pre-compliant with EU Battery Regulation requirements, eliminating the regulatory risk premium affecting 70% of global supply.

⚠ Risk Flags

Most cobalt trades on long-term contracts rather than spot markets, requiring Canadian producers to secure multi-year agreements rather than opportunistic sales. The EU Battery Regulation's 2025 implementation creates a compliance cliff that could disrupt existing supply chains, potentially benefiting Canadian suppliers but requiring proactive contract negotiation.

🧲 Rare Earth Elements

$58
→0.0% WoW   ▼18.8% YoY
SUPPLY SQUEEZE
Price Intelligence

At $58/kg, rare earths remain down 18.8% year-over-year, but Western buyers are paying 20-40% premiums over Chinese spot prices for non-Chinese supply. This premium structure creates immediate revenue upside for Canadian producers who can bypass Chinese processing — the SRC Saskatoon facility and Nechalacho mine position Canada to capture these premiums directly.

→ Canada's unique position as the only non-Chinese country with both operational rare earth mining and processing creates immediate access to 20-40% pricing premiums, with US DoD funding available this week for qualifying projects.

Demand Geography

The US DoD is actively funding Canadian rare earth projects under the Defense Production Act, requiring elimination of Chinese rare earth dependence by 2027 — representing ~5,000 tonnes NdPr oxide annually. Japan's JOGMEC is funding overseas projects through their Canadian office following lessons from China's 2010 embargo. The EU Critical Raw Materials Act mandates 40% processing from strategic partners like Canada by 2030, targeting ~4,000 tonnes NdPr oxide annually, while EV motor demand for neodymium-iron-boron magnets grows 25% yearly across all regions.

Supply Gap Analysis

China controls 85-90% of global rare earth processing and has active export controls on dysprosium, terbium, and neodymium since April 2025, creating critical supply gaps for Western magnet manufacturers like Vacuumschmelze and MP Materials. Only 2 commercial-scale separation facilities exist outside China globally — Canada's SRC Saskatoon facility represents 50% of non-Chinese processing capacity, positioning Canadian producers to fill structural deficits as EV demand accelerates.

🇨🇦 Canada's Competitive Position

Canada is the only non-Chinese country with both operational mine production (Nechalacho, NWT) and separation processing capacity (SRC Saskatoon), creating a complete supply chain free of Chinese ownership. The US DoD actively funds Canadian rare earth projects under defense partnerships, while Canadian REEs qualify as domestic content under US defense procurement and the Inflation Reduction Act through the Canada-US Critical Minerals Action Plan.

⚠ Risk Flags

Chinese export controls on key rare earths are likely to tighten further, creating both opportunity and supply chain disruption risk for Canadian processors relying on Chinese feedstock. Canadian producers must ensure complete supply chain independence from Chinese inputs to maintain premium pricing access.

Industrial and Technological Benefits (ITB) Policy
Value obligation equal to contract value (100% or greater)

When foreign companies win major Canadian defence contracts, ITB requires them to spend equivalent value in Canada — creating a direct business development channel for Canadian suppliers. Applies to all DND and PSPC contracts over $100M.

Best for: Smaller Canadian companies that want to get into a major prime contractor's supply chain. Primes like Lockheed Martin, Boeing, Rheinmetall, and BAE Systems all have active ITB obligations in Canada and are actively seeking qualified Canadian subcontractors.
