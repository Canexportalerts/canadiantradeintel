What We Think
This week marked a watershed moment for Canadian critical minerals exports with India's CEPA launch and $2.6B uranium deal fundamentally reshaping global market dynamics. The G7 Critical Minerals Production Alliance's $18.5B mobilization, combined with Indonesia's FIPA signing, positions Canadian producers at the center of Western supply chain diversification away from Chinese and Russian sources. While commodity prices remained stable, the institutional infrastructure for long-term Canadian mineral exports expanded dramatically, particularly in uranium and battery materials where geopolitical premiums are driving procurement decisions over pure price competition.

Government Primary Sources

  • Statistics Canada — Canadian International Merchandise Trade (CIMT) (Monthly — current through ~2 months prior) — Detailed monthly import and export data at the 6-digit HS commodity level. Selectable by country, province, year and month. Exportable to CSV. Best source for current bilateral goods trade flows by sector.
  • Statistics Canada — The Daily: International Merchandise Trade (Monthly — published with ~5 week lag) — Monthly press release covering Canada's merchandise trade with top 10 partners, NAPCS sector breakdown, and balance of payments data. Released approximately 5–6 weeks after reference month.
  • Statistics Canada — Canada and the World Statistics Hub (Varies by indicator; generally annual with some quarterly series) — Interactive bilateral data hub covering trade, investment, employment and travel for 18 of Canada's major trading partners. Brings together multiple StatCan datasets in a single country-level analytical tool.
  • ISED Canadian Importers Database (2023 (CBSA-sourced) — NOTE: typically lags ~2 years; 2024 not yet released as of April 2026) — Company-level list of major Canadian importers by product (HS code), city, and country of origin. 'Major importers' = companies collectively accounting for up to 80% of imports of a given product. Includes customs brokers and non-resident importers.
  • ISED Trade Data Online (Monthly — current through recent months) — Monthly aggregate bilateral trade flows for Canadian imports and exports, by HS product and NAICS industry, for 200+ countries. Sourced from Statistics Canada and U.S. Census Bureau.
  • Natural Resources Canada — Mineral Trade Statistics (Annual — data through April 2025, subject to revision) — Bilateral mineral and metals trade data by country and commodity, classified by stage of processing (raw → refined → manufactured). Derived from CBSA/StatCan customs data, further annotated by NRCan by mineral type. Current through April 2025.
  • NRCan — Canadian Critical Minerals Strategy Progress Update (Current — last updated February 2026) — Live government progress page covering G7 Critical Minerals Action Plan outcomes, bilateral agreements (US, UK, EU, France, Germany, Japan, Australia, Chile, South Korea), Investment Canada Act amendments, stockpiling initiatives, and the Global Partnerships Initiative. Updated February 2026.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's State of Trade (2024 full-year (2025 report)) — Annual comprehensive report on Canada's international trade and economic performance. Covers merchandise exports/imports, services trade, FDI flows in and out, sector-by-sector performance, and the economic outlook. 2025 edition covers full-year 2024 data.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's Response to U.S. Tariffs (Current — actively updated) — Live policy page covering Canada's response to U.S. tariffs including relief measures, support programs, retaliatory measures, and sector impacts. Frequently updated — key cross-sector risk signal.
  • EDC — Canada's Cleantech Future: Scalable, Profitable, Essential (2025) (October 2025) Free EDC account registration required for full report. Executive summary accessible without login. — EDC's flagship annual cleantech report identifying Canada's three core export advantages: renewable energy, EVs/electrified transport, and electrification technologies. Key finding: 77% of Canada's cleantech exports go to the U.S., creating concentration risk. Global clean energy investment: US$2 trillion in 2024.

1. Situation Summary

Canada's C$145 million lithium investment and imminent India-Canada CEPA completion create a 12-month window to capture downstream battery supply chain partnerships before U.S. and Asian competitors consolidate market share. Canadian miners and equipment suppliers must act immediately on India market access and secure supply chain compliance with updated export controls to capitalize on federal momentum.

2. Top Signals
Canada Growth Fund deployed C$145 million into lithium mine expansion, signaling federal strategy to build domestic battery supply chains.
This capital injection creates immediate partnership opportunities for Canadian equipment suppliers and junior miners to integrate into expanded operations before international competitors enter.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
India-Canada CEPA completion targeted for year-end 2026 would unlock Cameco's C$2.6 billion uranium deal and tariff reductions across critical minerals.
India scores 9/10 opportunity but only 2/10 regulatory clarity; CEPA completion removes tariff barriers and creates market access for junior miners currently shut out.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
Export Control List updated Q1 2025 with revised HS code classifications for critical minerals.
Compliance failures trigger OFAC penalties and shipment seizures; verification must occur before next export to avoid disruption.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
China rare earth export control review scheduled April 2026 may further restrict Canadian buyer access to additional elements.
Restriction cascade would force Canadian manufacturers to accelerate domestic sourcing and supply chain diversification away from Chinese inputs.
CONFIDENCE: MED
CUSMA Joint Review in July 2026 will test U.S. positioning on critical minerals rules of origin.
Rule tightening could exclude Canadian lithium and uranium from preferential trade access, making India and Indonesia partnerships strategically critical.
CONFIDENCE: MED
3. Recommended Actions
01Register immediately with Trade Commissioner Service India desk for the Toronto Canada-India Critical Minerals Dialogue (inaugural session, March 2026 deadline March 10).
This is the first formal bilateral critical minerals forum; early participation secures introductions to Indian government buyers and uranium/lithium importers before CEPA negotiation closes.
02Audit supply chains for Russian metals sector exposure and document findings against OFAC EO-14024 sectoral designation requirements.
Any entity in Russia's metals and mining sector triggers sanctions; non-compliance blocks U.S. and allied market access and creates liability for Canadian exporters.
03Submit applications to NRCan's C$2 billion Critical Minerals Sovereign Fund before March 31 fiscal year-end deadline.
Rolling application window closes annually; funding supports downstream processing, equipment manufacturing, and supply chain infrastructure tied to lithium and uranium expansion.
