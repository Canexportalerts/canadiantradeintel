China is Canada's second-largest trading partner by total bilateral volume — a position achieved despite no free trade agreement, significant diplomatic friction since 2018, and a conscious Canadian policy effort to reduce dependency. The $118B relationship is deeply asymmetric: Canada imports nearly $89B from China while exporting only $29B in return. The deficit is large, widening, and concentrated in categories where Canada has limited short-term substitution options.

The Canada–China relationship has been operating under significant diplomatic strain since the arrest of Meng Wanzhou in 2018 and the subsequent detention of Michael Kovrig and Michael Spavor. Relations normalized somewhat after Meng's release in 2021, but the relationship remains fundamentally altered — more transactional, less institutionally deep, and increasingly shaped by Canada's broader alignment with Western democracies on China-related security concerns.

Key Canadian exports: Canola (China was historically Canada's largest canola customer before 2019 restrictions), pulses, lumber, coal, potash, seafood, aircraft parts.

Key Canadian imports from China: Consumer electronics, machinery, clothing and textiles, furniture, chemicals, plastics, solar panels, EV batteries and components.

02

Market Opportunity

Despite the diplomatic complexity, China remains a significant export opportunity for specific Canadian sectors — primarily agri-food and natural resources. The commercial reality is that Chinese demand for Canadian canola, pulses, lumber, and potash is large and relatively price-inelastic. Canadian producers benefit when the relationship is stable and face significant market disruption when it is not.

Agri-food — Cautious Opportunity
China was Canada's largest canola export market before the 2019 restrictions. Chinese demand for Canadian canola oil, pulses, pork, and seafood remains strong. The market opportunity is real but subject to disruption from diplomatic tensions — Canadian agri-food exporters should maintain China market relationships while actively developing alternatives.
Critical Minerals — Competitive Tension
China dominates global critical minerals processing — Canada is simultaneously a supplier of raw minerals to China and a competitor for downstream processing capacity. The strategic dynamic is complex: selling Canadian minerals to China for processing, then buying back refined materials, is a dependency Canadian policy is actively trying to address through domestic processing investment.
Potash — Stable Demand
China is the world's largest potash consumer (fertilizer for agriculture). Canadian potash producers — Nutrien, Mosaic — have multi-decade supply relationships with Chinese agricultural buyers. This trade relationship has been less affected by diplomatic tensions than other sectors, reflecting China's genuine agricultural need for Canadian potash.
Diversification Opportunity
Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy and trade diversification agenda represent a deliberate effort to reduce China dependency in both directions — developing alternative export markets and alternative sources for imports currently dominated by China. This creates opportunities for Canadian businesses that can serve markets previously served by China-based supply chains.
03

Import Dependency

Canada's $88.8B in Chinese imports represents a structural dependency that is the central strategic challenge in the bilateral relationship. Canadian businesses and consumers rely on Chinese manufacturing for a wide range of products — from consumer electronics to solar panels to pharmaceutical inputs — where near-term substitution is difficult and expensive.

The categories of greatest strategic concern are pharmaceutical active ingredients (API), rare earth elements used in defence systems and clean energy, and critical minerals processing capacity. These are areas where Canada has identified supply chain vulnerability and is actively investing in alternatives — but diversification takes years, not months.

High-dependency categories: Consumer electronics (smartphones, laptops, televisions), clothing and textiles, solar panels and clean energy equipment, pharmaceutical APIs, EV batteries and components, rare earth magnets, and a wide range of industrial machinery.

The diversification challenge: Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy commits to supply chain diversification away from single-source dependencies. But for many of these categories, China's manufacturing scale, cost, and speed advantages have no immediate equivalent — diversification requires sustained policy support, capital investment, and multi-year lead times. 05

Trade Agreement Coverage

No FTAWTO Only
Canada and China trade under WTO most-favoured-nation terms only. No bilateral free trade agreement exists and none is currently being negotiated. Canada explored an FTA with China in the 2017–2018 period but negotiations were suspended amid political tensions over Canada's handling of the Meng Wanzhou case and never resumed.
What this means: Canadian goods entering China face China's WTO MFN tariff rates — generally moderate for industrial goods but higher for agricultural products. Canadian companies have no special procurement access to Chinese government contracts. Investment protections are limited to what China has agreed under WTO investment commitments — less than Canadian companies would have under a formal investment treaty.
China's CPTPP application: China has applied to join CPTPP — an outcome that would fundamentally reshape Canadian trade policy toward China. Current CPTPP members have not reached consensus on China's bid, and Japan's opposition is significant. This remains a long-term watch item. See CPTPP expansion watch
06

Canadian Government Signals

📄
Indo-Pacific Strategy · Global Affairs Canada · 2022
Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy describes China as presenting "an increasingly disruptive global power" while maintaining that engagement remains necessary. The strategy explicitly commits to "competing where we must and cooperating where we can" — a structured ambiguity that reflects genuine policy tension between economic and security interests.
📄
Critical Minerals Strategy · NRCan · 2022
Canada's Critical Minerals Strategy explicitly identifies reduction of dependency on Chinese processing capacity as a strategic priority — committing to domestic value-added processing of Canadian critical minerals to reduce the "mine in Canada, process in China, buy back from China" cycle.
📄
EV Tariffs and Steel Tariffs · Finance Canada · 2024
Canada's 2024 announcement of 100% tariffs on Chinese electric vehicles and 25% tariffs on Chinese steel and aluminum marked a significant escalation in Canada's willingness to use trade policy tools against China — aligning with US and EU actions and signalling a structural shift away from the engagement-first approach of the prior decade.
