What We Think
— CTI Editorial Desk  ·  May 12, 2026

What the signals taken together reveal this week is that Canada is simultaneously the world's most advantaged agricultural supplier and its own worst logistics enemy. The USDA WASDE supply cut, the US drought, the Middle East risk premium on energy and oilseeds, India's pulse import dependence on Canadian lentils, and record Canadian inventory all point in the same direction: Canadian commodities are price-makers, not price-takers, for the first time in years. But the Anderson Economic Group study is a cold bucket of water. A $540 million per week loss figure for port and rail disruptions means that a single two-week labour dispute during this export window would erase more value than the entire AgriMarketing Market Diversification program could generate in years. That is the structural contradiction that no individual headline captures. What Canadian businesses in this sector are most likely getting wrong is treating the logistics risk and the export opportunity as separate problems managed by separate teams. Viterra and Richardson International have both the export contracts to capture and the port exposure to lose. Canola processors with EU off-take agreements under CETA need throughput certainty at Vancouver, not just price certainty at ICE Futures. The companies that will capture this window are those that hedge the logistics risk - through contract clauses, diversified routing through Thunder Bay for Great Lakes export, and active engagement with the Agriculture Transport Coalition's lobbying campaign - at the same time they are locking forward sales. The evidence points toward a 60-day window of export pricing advantage that closes when US drought conditions are either confirmed as catastrophic (extending the window) or partially relieved by late-spring precipitation (compressing it sharply). Canadian exporters should treat the second Friday of June - the next USDA WASDE release - as the decision deadline for Q3 contract positioning. Any business not in market before that report is speculating on the outcome rather than managing it.
