· Mining Indigenous Partnership This Quarter
Indigenous communities increasingly shift from traditional Impact Benefit Agreements toward direct equity ownership in m
Indigenous communities across Canada are increasingly moving away from traditional impact benefit agreements (IBAs) toward direct equity ownership in mining and energy projects, with a strategic report released last month by Thunder Bay-based Waawoono Consultancy challenging the mining industry's reliance on fixed-payment structures. The report argues that emerging Indigenous financing tools, including the Canada Indigenous Loan Guarantee Corporation, are accelerating this transformation from consultation to ownership, with Nations shifting from being stakeholders who receive payments to partners who exercise boardroom-level influence over project decisions.
What This Could Mean For YouThis shift toward Indigenous equity ownership could accelerate project approvals and reduce regulatory uncertainty for Canadian mining companies, while creating new partnership structures that may require different financing and operational approaches for businesses seeking to develop resources on Indigenous territories.

· Mining Processing Refining Immediate
Mangrove Lithium opened North America's first commercial electrochemical lithium refinery in Delta, BC in April 2026, pr
Canadian startup Mangrove Lithium opened its first commercial lithium refinery in Delta, British Columbia, converting spodumene concentrate into high-purity, battery-grade lithium hydroxide at the site. The plant will initially produce 1,000 tonnes of battery-grade lithium hydroxide per year, enough for 25,000 electric vehicles, with plans for a second facility with 20,000 tonnes capacity annually sufficient for 500,000 electric vehicles. Canadian Minister of Energy and Natural Resources Tim Hodgson has publicly framed the facility as representative of sovereign industrial projects Canada needs, with critical minerals strategy becoming a central pillar of Canadian industrial policy under Prime Minister Mark Carney's government.
What This Could Mean For YouThis represents a breakthrough in Canadian midstream processing capacity, reducing dependence on Chinese lithium refining and creating domestic supply chain security for battery manufacturers. Canadian exporters gain access to value-added lithium products while importers of battery materials have new domestic sourcing options.

Sources & References

Canadian Trade Intelligence aggregates and synthesizes primary government data, Crown corporation intelligence, and vetted news sources to produce this report. We are the bridge between Canada's government data infrastructure and the actionable weekly intelligence your business needs. All primary sources are publicly accessible and linked below.

Government Primary Sources

  • Statistics Canada — Canadian International Merchandise Trade (CIMT) (Monthly — current through ~2 months prior) — Detailed monthly import and export data at the 6-digit HS commodity level. Selectable by country, province, year and month. Exportable to CSV. Best source for current bilateral goods trade flows by sector.
  • Statistics Canada — The Daily: International Merchandise Trade (Monthly — published with ~5 week lag) — Monthly press release covering Canada's merchandise trade with top 10 partners, NAPCS sector breakdown, and balance of payments data. Released approximately 5–6 weeks after reference month.
  • Statistics Canada — Canada and the World Statistics Hub (Varies by indicator; generally annual with some quarterly series) — Interactive bilateral data hub covering trade, investment, employment and travel for 18 of Canada's major trading partners. Brings together multiple StatCan datasets in a single country-level analytical tool.
  • ISED Canadian Importers Database (2023 (CBSA-sourced) — NOTE: typically lags ~2 years; 2024 not yet released as of April 2026) — Company-level list of major Canadian importers by product (HS code), city, and country of origin. 'Major importers' = companies collectively accounting for up to 80% of imports of a given product. Includes customs brokers and non-resident importers.
  • ISED Trade Data Online (Monthly — current through recent months) — Monthly aggregate bilateral trade flows for Canadian imports and exports, by HS product and NAICS industry, for 200+ countries. Sourced from Statistics Canada and U.S. Census Bureau.
  • Natural Resources Canada — Mineral Trade Statistics (Annual — data through April 2025, subject to revision) — Bilateral mineral and metals trade data by country and commodity, classified by stage of processing (raw → refined → manufactured). Derived from CBSA/StatCan customs data, further annotated by NRCan by mineral type. Current through April 2025.
  • NRCan — Canadian Critical Minerals Strategy Progress Update (Current — last updated February 2026) — Live government progress page covering G7 Critical Minerals Action Plan outcomes, bilateral agreements (US, UK, EU, France, Germany, Japan, Australia, Chile, South Korea), Investment Canada Act amendments, stockpiling initiatives, and the Global Partnerships Initiative. Updated February 2026.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's State of Trade (2024 full-year (2025 report)) — Annual comprehensive report on Canada's international trade and economic performance. Covers merchandise exports/imports, services trade, FDI flows in and out, sector-by-sector performance, and the economic outlook. 2025 edition covers full-year 2024 data.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's Response to U.S. Tariffs (Current — actively updated) — Live policy page covering Canada's response to U.S. tariffs including relief measures, support programs, retaliatory measures, and sector impacts. Frequently updated — key cross-sector risk signal.
  • EDC — Canada's Cleantech Future: Scalable, Profitable, Essential (2025) (October 2025) Free EDC account registration required for full report. Executive summary accessible without login. — EDC's flagship annual cleantech report identifying Canada's three core export advantages: renewable energy, EVs/electrified transport, and electrification technologies. Key finding: 77% of Canada's cleantech exports go to the U.S., creating concentration risk. Global clean energy investment: US$2 trillion in 2024.

⚠️ Importer data note: Company-level import intelligence sourced from the ISED Canadian Importers Database. Most recent available data is 2023 (CBSA-sourced). 2024 data not yet released as of April 2026. For current aggregate trade flows by product and country, see StatCan CIMT (updated monthly).

Industry & Crown Corporation Sources

  • NRCan — Critical Minerals Strategy Annual Report 2024 (2024 annual) — Covers supply chain diversification progress, bilateral critical minerals agreements, G7 cooperation frameworks, and the $70M Global Partnerships Initiative. Key data: refined material production concentration by mineral.

2. Top Signals
Canada-India Critical Minerals Dialogue inaugural session launches in Toronto this March with early registration required.
India scores 9/10 opportunity but 2/10 regulatory risk, making this dialogue critical to navigate market entry and establish preferred-partner status before US positioning hardens in July CUSMA review.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
OFAC sectoral designation EO-14024 now covers all Russian metals and mining entities without carve-outs.
Any supply chain exposure to Russian metals operators triggers US secondary sanctions; Canadian firms must audit vendors immediately to avoid export license revocation.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
Export Control List updated Q1 2025 with revised HS code classifications for critical minerals.
Misclassified shipments face detention and penalties; reclassification may trigger permit requirements for previously unrestricted goods.
CONFIDENCE: MED
China is expected to expand rare earth export controls in April 2026 review, potentially restricting additional elements.
Tighter Chinese controls will increase Canadian buyer demand for alternative suppliers and create hedging opportunities for domestic and allied producers.
CONFIDENCE: MED
