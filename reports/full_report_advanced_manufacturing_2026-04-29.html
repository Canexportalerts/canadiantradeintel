Government Primary Sources

  • Statistics Canada — Canadian International Merchandise Trade (CIMT) (Monthly — current through ~2 months prior) — Detailed monthly import and export data at the 6-digit HS commodity level. Selectable by country, province, year and month. Exportable to CSV. Best source for current bilateral goods trade flows by sector.
  • Statistics Canada — The Daily: International Merchandise Trade (Monthly — published with ~5 week lag) — Monthly press release covering Canada's merchandise trade with top 10 partners, NAPCS sector breakdown, and balance of payments data. Released approximately 5–6 weeks after reference month.
  • Statistics Canada — Canada and the World Statistics Hub (Varies by indicator; generally annual with some quarterly series) — Interactive bilateral data hub covering trade, investment, employment and travel for 18 of Canada's major trading partners. Brings together multiple StatCan datasets in a single country-level analytical tool.
  • ISED Canadian Importers Database (2023 (CBSA-sourced) — NOTE: typically lags ~2 years; 2024 not yet released as of April 2026) — Company-level list of major Canadian importers by product (HS code), city, and country of origin. 'Major importers' = companies collectively accounting for up to 80% of imports of a given product. Includes customs brokers and non-resident importers.
  • ISED Trade Data Online (Monthly — current through recent months) — Monthly aggregate bilateral trade flows for Canadian imports and exports, by HS product and NAICS industry, for 200+ countries. Sourced from Statistics Canada and U.S. Census Bureau.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's State of Trade (2024 full-year (2025 report)) — Annual comprehensive report on Canada's international trade and economic performance. Covers merchandise exports/imports, services trade, FDI flows in and out, sector-by-sector performance, and the economic outlook. 2025 edition covers full-year 2024 data.
  • Global Affairs Canada — Canada's Response to U.S. Tariffs (Current — actively updated) — Live policy page covering Canada's response to U.S. tariffs including relief measures, support programs, retaliatory measures, and sector impacts. Frequently updated — key cross-sector risk signal.
  • CanadaBuys — Government of Canada Procurement (Real-time) — Official Government of Canada procurement portal. Covers federal tender notices, awarded contracts, and standing offers. Key for Aerospace & Defence and Tech sectors.

⚠️ Importer data note: Company-level import intelligence sourced from the ISED Canadian Importers Database. Most recent available data is 2023 (CBSA-sourced). 2024 data not yet released as of April 2026. For current aggregate trade flows by product and country, see StatCan CIMT (updated monthly).

1. Situation Summary

The U.S. has opened a Section 232 tariff relief pathway for Canadian steel and aluminum producers willing to commit capital to U.S. facilities, creating a time-bound decision point for advanced manufacturers on whether U.S. investment or Mexico-based supply chains deliver better returns. CUSMA tariff negotiations through May will determine whether copper and downstream metals face additional duties, making supply chain repositioning urgent for manufacturers with U.S. exposure.

2. Top Signals
U.S. Department of Commerce announced April 27 that Canadian steel and aluminum producers can reduce Section 232 tariffs through direct U.S. capital commitments.
This creates an immediate comparative analysis opportunity for Canadian producers deciding between U.S. facility investment, Mexican nearshoring, or domestic capacity expansion under Strategic Innovation Fund support.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
Mexico has extended a parallel tariff reduction offer, positioning itself as a competing nearshoring destination.
Canadian manufacturers must evaluate ROI across three jurisdictions within weeks, requiring rapid access to trade finance and site assessment capabilities.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH
CUSMA tariff negotiations continue through May with potential for duty expansion on copper and downstream metals.
Any further duties will force immediate supply chain repositioning; manufacturers should not finalize sourcing decisions until May tariff decisions are published.
CONFIDENCE: MED
EU markets (Germany, European Union) are scoring 8/10 opportunity with only 2/10 regulatory risk, versus U.S. at 9/10 opportunity but 3/10 risk.
Advanced manufacturers with dual-market capability should accelerate EU alignment investments now, as tariff volatility makes North American market positioning unstable.
CONFIDENCE: MED
3. Recommended Actions
01Activate Trade Commissioner Service Advanced Manufacturing Network alerts and contact EDC within 48 hours to model Section 232 investment scenarios against Mexico alternatives.
Trade commissioners hold real-time Section 232 exemption data; EDC political risk insurance is prerequisite for committing capital to U.S. facilities.
02Submit Strategic Innovation Fund capacity-building applications immediately for any advanced manufacturing expansion planned before June, as nearshoring demand signals create window of competitive advantage.
SIF approvals take 8-12 weeks; delaying past May risks losing capacity-building grants as U.S. and Mexico demand accelerates.
03Defer final sourcing commitments on copper and downstream metals until May 15 CUSMA tariff decision window closes.
Supply chain repositioning triggered by tariff expansion will be more expensive mid-project; wait for decision clarity before locking supplier contracts.
