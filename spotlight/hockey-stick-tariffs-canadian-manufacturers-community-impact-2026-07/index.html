Hockey Stick Makers Caught in a Trade War That Feels Personal to Canadians

Share LinkedIn X

In Drummondville, Quebec, a modest industrial park houses one of the last remaining hockey stick manufacturers in Canada still producing for the North American mass market. Workers there have spent the past decade watching production migrate to Asia. Now, the plant faces a new threat — not from cheaper labour overseas, but from the country that invented the sport's most passionate consumer base.

The United States has imposed steep new tariffs on Canadian hockey sticks, part of a broadening trade dispute that also covers dairy and alcohol. The move targets a product so tied to Canadian identity that it borders on the symbolic. But for the workers and small-business owners in communities like Drummondville, Sherbrooke, and Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu — where manufacturing remains a significant source of middle-income employment — the symbolism is secondary to the payroll.

Canadian hockey stick producers have long relied on U.S. market access to achieve the volume that makes domestic manufacturing viable. The U.S. is not just a trading partner for this industry; it is the margin. Youth leagues, recreational players, and NHL equipment suppliers south of the border represent a customer base that Canadian manufacturers cannot replicate at home. Canada's population simply isn't large enough.

The tariffs arrive at a fragile moment. Several Quebec and Ontario producers have only recently reinvested in composite manufacturing technology to compete on quality rather than price. Those capital expenditures were predicated on stable U.S. market access. With steep new duties in place, the business case for keeping production in Canada weakens considerably — and the temptation to shift final assembly to a tariff-exempt jurisdiction grows.

For the communities affected, this is about more than trade policy. Hockey stick manufacturing is one of those rare remaining industries where the product, the workforce, and the national identity feel genuinely connected. Plant closures or production shifts would register as cultural losses, not just economic ones.

Local union reps say they've been through this before — NAFTA renegotiations, Buy American provisions, COVID supply shocks — and that each time, the industry shrinks a little more. This round, they say, feels different. The tariff rate is higher, the political environment in Washington is less predictable, and Ottawa's leverage is uncertain.

What happens next in Drummondville may say something larger about whether Canada can hold onto the manufacturing base that makes some of its most Canadian things.
