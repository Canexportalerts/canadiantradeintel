· Supply Chain Risk Immediate
Canada-China preliminary trade agreement reached in January 2026 reduces tariffs and addresses supply chain dependencies
Canada and China signed a preliminary trade agreement in January 2026, with Canada permitting up to 49,000 Chinese EVs annually under 6.1% tariff (down from 100%) and China reducing canola seed duties from 84% to 15% by March 1, 2026. The agreement affects $4 billion in annual canola trade and includes tariff relief on steel, aluminum, lobster, crab, and peas.
What This Means For YouCanadian manufacturers gain access to affordable Chinese components and materials while agricultural exporters regain critical market access. Joint venture opportunities with Chinese companies in EV and battery sectors may drive new investment in Canadian manufacturing.
Source: MLT Aikins
· Industrial Policy Immediate
Canada launched its first Defence Industrial Strategy on February 17, 2026, prioritizing Canadian suppliers and establis
Prime Minister Carney launched Canada's Defence Industrial Strategy with $81.8 billion in funding through 2025, implementing a 'Build-Partner-Buy' framework. The government simultaneously implemented the Buy Canadian Policy on December 16, 2025, prioritizing Canadian suppliers for contracts over $25 million (expanding to $5 million by spring 2026). CME launched CME Defence initiative on February 19, 2026, to help manufacturers access defence supply chains.
What This Means For YouThese policies represent the most significant shift toward protectionist industrial policy in decades, directly benefiting Canadian manufacturers through guaranteed domestic preference in federal procurement. The Defence Industrial Strategy alone could create up to 125,000 jobs and drive sustained demand for Canadian suppliers in strategic sectors.
Source: Prime Minister of Canada
· Steel Aluminum Input Costs Immediate
As of March 12, 2025, all imports of steel and aluminum are subject to a 25% tariff, representin
All previous Section 232 steel and aluminum country exemptions and tariff-rate quota deals with Canada, Mexico, the European Union, Japan, South Korea, Australia and others have been eliminated. In June 2025, President Trump increased steel and aluminum tariffs to 50%, except for imports from the United Kingdom. Manufacturers of automobiles, appliances, construction materials, packaging, and countless other products that use steel or aluminum as inputs have faced increased costs.
Risk AssessmentCanadian manufacturers including Magna, Martinrea, and Linamar now face increased input costs as Canada lost its previous exemptions under CUSMA/USMCA. All Canadian steel and aluminum exports to the US now face the full tariff burden, creating material cost pressure for integrated supply chains.
Source: Auto Care Association

· Mexico Competitor This Quarter
Mexico's foreign direct investment surged 10% to $34.3 billion in H1 2025, with 36% flowing to manufacturing, driven by
Mexico received $34.3 billion in FDI in the first half of 2025, a 10% year-over-year increase. The US contributed $15 billion while Canada, Spain, Germany, Japan, South Korea and China were significant investors. 36% of capital flowed into manufacturing, with transport equipment accounting for nearly half of manufacturing FDI. COMCE projects Mexico's exports approaching $700 billion with 6.5% growth in 2026.
Risk AssessmentMexican manufacturing momentum challenges Canada's ability to compete for North American investment and export opportunities, particularly as the USMCA review approaches in July 2026. Mexico's success in attracting diverse international investment, including Canadian capital, highlights the competitive pressure Canadian manufacturers face in nearshoring decisions.
Source: Global Trade Magazine
