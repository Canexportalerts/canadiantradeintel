01

Overview

Vietnam has been Canada's largest trading partner in the ASEAN region since 2015 and is the CPTPP's standout bilateral success story for Canada. Since the CPTPP entered into force in Vietnam in January 2019, Canada-Vietnam merchandise trade has grown by 117% — the largest CPTPP-driven trade growth of any bilateral pairing — rising from approximately C$6.5 billion in 2018 to an estimated C$14–15 billion by 2024. This growth has been driven primarily by a surge in Canadian imports of Vietnamese electronics, textiles, footwear, and furniture — goods where Vietnam has become a globally competitive manufacturer benefiting from supply chain shifts away from China.

The strategic significance of Vietnam to Canadian trade intelligence extends well beyond bilateral goods flows. Vietnam has emerged as one of the world's most important supply chain diversification destinations, absorbing manufacturing capacity, foreign direct investment, and supply chain relationships that were previously concentrated in China. Major electronics producers — Samsung, Intel, LG, Foxconn — have built enormous manufacturing bases in Vietnam, and Vietnam now accounts for a significant share of global smartphone, semiconductor packaging, and consumer electronics production. Canadian importers sourcing electronics components, garments, footwear, or furniture have supply chain exposures to Vietnam whether or not those relationships are explicitly managed as Vietnam strategies.

Vietnam's economic trajectory is one of the most consistent in Asia: GDP growth has averaged 6–7% annually over the past decade, the country has reduced poverty to near-single-digit rates, and a young, urban, and increasingly consumption-driven middle class is emerging. For Canadian exporters, Vietnam represents both a production platform (manufacturing goods for export to Canada) and an end market (Vietnamese consumers purchasing Canadian agri-food, education, and technology products). The CPTPP framework governs both directions of this relationship.

Vietnam's political system is a one-party state governed by the Communist Party of Vietnam (CPV), providing a high degree of policy continuity and stability that has been a consistent feature of Vietnam's three-decade economic transformation. General Secretary To Lam assumed the party's top position in 2024, continuing the CPV's pragmatic economic development orientation. The Vietnamese government's approach — firm ideological control combined with aggressive FDI attraction and market-oriented economic management — has created a distinctive political economy that functions effectively as an investment and manufacturing destination despite its non-democratic structure.

Vietnam's foreign policy doctrine — "Bamboo Diplomacy" — positions the country as strategically equidistant between great powers, maintaining deep economic relationships with both the United States (now a Comprehensive Strategic Partner since 2023) and China (its largest trading partner) simultaneously. For Canadian businesses, Vietnam's neutral positioning means commercial relationships operate without the geopolitical friction that would characterize engagement in more contested bilateral contexts. The CPV's ongoing anti-corruption campaign ("burning furnace") has resulted in significant turnover among senior officials, creating short-term uncertainty in government contracting and procurement relationships in regulated sectors.

Vietnam's economic performance is among the most consistent in Asia. GDP growth of 7.1% in 2024 reflected strong export performance, continued FDI inflows, and resilient domestic consumption — maintaining Vietnam's position as one of the world's fastest-growing large economies. The manufacturing sector anchored by electronics production (Samsung alone accounts for approximately 20% of Vietnam's total export value), textiles, footwear, and furniture is the engine of Vietnam's export growth, while a growing services sector and urbanizing consumer base are building a domestic demand foundation.

Vietnam has successfully graduated from a low-cost labour competitor to a medium-complexity manufacturing hub. Average manufacturing wages have risen significantly but remain competitive relative to China, and Vietnam's young, literate, and increasingly technically trained workforce can support mid-complexity electronics assembly, precision manufacturing, and software services. Vietnam supplies approximately 35% of Canada's shrimp imports — a concrete supply chain dependency that is operationally significant for Canadian seafood importers. The government's strategic ambition to move Vietnam into semiconductor packaging, EV components, and advanced manufacturing by 2030 requires sustained FDI and technology transfer from global companies.

Top Canadian exports to Vietnam: Canadian exports are led by agri-food products benefiting from CPTPP tariff elimination — canola seed (zero tariff upon entry into force), beef, pork, and wheat are the primary agricultural exports. Canada has eliminated 94% of tariff lines for Vietnamese imports; Vietnam has eliminated approximately 66% of tariff lines for Canadian imports with phase-in periods for sensitive agricultural sectors. Beyond agri-food, Canadian exports include aerospace components, industrial machinery, and educational services (Vietnamese students in Canada represent a significant bilateral services trade category).

Top Canadian imports from Vietnam: Electronics and electrical equipment dominate, reflecting Vietnam's position as a major Samsung, Intel, and LG manufacturing hub. Canadian imports of Vietnamese electronics — smartphones, consumer electronics components, and semiconductor packaging — are often embedded in supply chains where Vietnam's role as production location is not immediately visible. Textiles and garments (Vietnam is the world's third-largest apparel exporter), footwear and leather goods, furniture, and seafood (shrimp, tra fish) are other significant import categories. The 117% growth since CPTPP implementation reflects both tariff-driven trade creation and the broader structural shift in global manufacturing geography toward Vietnam.

05

Market Access

CPTPP
In Force — Canada: Dec 2018 · Vietnam: Jan 2019
The Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership governs Canada-Vietnam trade and creates a distinctive bilateral market access architecture. Canada eliminated 94% of tariff lines for Vietnamese imports. Vietnam is phasing in tariff reductions for Canadian exports on a product-specific schedule: beef and pork tariffs phase out over 7–9 years, canola oil over 7 years. The full benefit for Canadian agri-food exporters to Vietnam will be realized over the 2025–2030 timeframe as phase-in schedules complete. CPTPP's IP, labour, environment, and services chapters provide enforceable standards for Canadian companies operating in Vietnam.
Full CPTPP guide — Canada coverage

Vietnam's regulatory environment for Canadian businesses requires active navigation. Foreign investment is welcomed but channelled through specific legal structures (100% foreign-owned enterprises, joint ventures, or business cooperation contracts) that require local legal advisory support. Vietnam's business licensing and regulatory approval processes can be time-consuming; local partnerships accelerate market entry. The Vietnam Chamber of Commerce and Industry (VCCI), the Canada-Vietnam Joint Economic Committee, and TCS Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City offices provide institutional navigation support.

⚠ Monitor
US tariff enforcement actions on Vietnam-origin goods — historically triggered by transshipment concerns about Chinese goods re-routed through Vietnam — create indirect risk for Canadian importers with Vietnamese manufacturing supply chains. Monitor US Commerce Department Section 301 and 232 developments for Vietnam-specific action.

Opportunity Assessment

Supply Chain Sourcing & Manufacturing · STRONG
Vietnam is the premier China-plus-one manufacturing destination in Southeast Asia. Canadian importers across retail, electronics, apparel, and industrial goods have active or latent Vietnam supply chain exposure. CPTPP's tariff elimination and rules of origin framework provides a structured basis for Canadian companies to formalize Vietnam sourcing, qualify Vietnamese suppliers for preferential tariff treatment, and build supply chain resilience through geographic diversification.
Agri-food Exports · STRONG
CPTPP's tariff elimination schedule for Canadian agri-food products into Vietnam creates a concrete export opportunity expanding as phase-in schedules complete. Vietnam's growing middle class — with rising incomes and protein consumption — is a natural market for Canadian beef, pork, canola oil, wheat, and premium food products. Vietnam currently supplies approximately 35% of Canada's shrimp imports, and this bilateral agri-food relationship has both import (seafood) and export (grains, oilseeds, protein) dimensions.
Education Services · STRONG
Vietnam is one of the world's largest sources of international students, and Canada is among the top destinations for Vietnamese students (90,000+). Education is a significant bilateral services trade category and creates a durable people-to-people link that facilitates broader commercial relationships. Canadian universities, colleges, and professional training institutions have active Vietnamese student recruitment and partnership programmes.
Technology & Digital Infrastructure · EMERGING
Vietnam's government has articulated a "digital Vietnam" agenda, targeting 20% of GDP from the digital economy by 2030. ICT currently contributes approximately 15% of GDP, and Vietnam's ambitions in semiconductor packaging, electronics manufacturing, and software services create demand for Canadian technology partnerships, training, and digital infrastructure. EDC opened its first Vietnam office in 2024 specifically to support Canadian exporters and investors navigating this opportunity.
Clean Energy & Infrastructure · EMERGING
Vietnam's energy transition — moving from coal dependency toward renewables — requires significant foreign investment and technology transfer. The country's offshore wind potential is among Asia's largest, and solar capacity expansion is underway. Canadian clean energy companies, project developers, and infrastructure investors have Vietnam market opportunities with the CPTPP framework, EDC's local presence, and the Vietnamese government's FDI attraction agenda providing multiple market entry channels.
Importers: Vietnamese Goods into Canada · VERY ACTIVE
The dominant direction of Canada-Vietnam trade is Vietnamese exports to Canada. Canadian retailers, electronics importers, apparel buyers, footwear distributors, and seafood importers are the most active players in the bilateral relationship. Companies not yet utilizing CPTPP rules of origin for Vietnamese imports should audit their sourcing for duty savings opportunities on the 94% of tariff lines Canada has eliminated for Vietnamese-origin goods.
07

Canadian Business Presence

Canadian business presence in Vietnam has grown significantly since CPTPP implementation in 2019, catalyzed by the agreement's market access provisions and Vietnam's position as a priority market under Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy. The Trade Commissioner Service maintains offices in both Hanoi and Ho Chi Minh City, reflecting Vietnam's unique two-city commercial geography — Hanoi as the political capital and government engagement hub, Ho Chi Minh City as the commercial and financial centre. Export Development Canada opened its first dedicated Vietnam office in March 2024, specifically to support Canadian exporters and investors managing Vietnam market exposure.

Canadian agri-food companies — including canola oil processors, pulse exporters, and grain traders — are the most commercially active Canadian exporters in Vietnam, benefiting from CPTPP tariff reductions. Several Canadian seafood importers and distributors have developed direct sourcing relationships with Vietnamese shrimp and seafood processors, with CPTPP's rules of origin framework providing the regulatory foundation. Canada has eliminated tariffs for Vietnamese seafood imports, making Vietnamese processors active participants in the Canadian food supply chain.

Canadian educational institutions — universities and colleges — are among the most visible Canadian brands in Vietnam through student recruitment and institutional partnerships. Multiple Canadian universities have Vietnamese alumni networks and active partner institution agreements. Vietnamese students in Canada (90,000+) represent a significant bilateral people-to-people connection that supports commercial relationship development well beyond the education sector itself.

RegulatoryModerateVietnam's regulatory environment for foreign business is improving but remains complex: licensing requirements, local content rules in certain sectors, land use restrictions, and evolving compliance standards. CPTPP's transparency and regulatory coherence chapters are gradually improving the environment for Canadian companies, but navigating Vietnamese bureaucratic processes requires local legal and operational support.
IP ProtectionModerateVietnam's CPTPP commitments have strengthened IP protection standards, but enforcement remains uneven. Counterfeiting and trademark infringement persist in consumer goods sectors. Canadian companies with significant IP exposure in Vietnam should establish trademark registrations and maintain active enforcement monitoring.
GeopoliticalModerateVietnam's Bamboo Diplomacy is structurally sound but creates exposure to spillover from US-China trade tensions. Procurement Pipeline

CPTPP includes government procurement provisions providing Canadian companies with improved access to Vietnamese public procurement above defined thresholds. Vietnam has committed to transparency in government procurement and progressive market opening under CPTPP's procurement chapter. For Canadian companies, Vietnam procurement opportunities are most relevant in infrastructure, energy, ICT, and healthcare sectors where government and state-owned enterprise spending is substantial.

🔗
Vietnam National Procurement Portal (Muasamcong)
Vietnam's national e-procurement system publishes government tender notices from central ministries, provincial authorities, and state-owned enterprises. Canadian companies can monitor procurement notices in priority sectors — infrastructure, healthcare equipment, energy, and ICT — and submit bids on open international tenders. Portal is in Vietnamese; TCS Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi provide navigation support. Access at muasamcong.mpi.gov.vn.
🔗
EVN — Vietnam Electricity Renewable Energy Programme
Vietnam Electricity (EVN) and the Ministry of Industry and Trade are managing Vietnam's ambitious renewable energy expansion — offshore wind, solar, and grid modernization. EVN procurement for generation equipment, transmission infrastructure, and grid management technology represents a sustained investment pipeline. Canadian clean energy companies and grid technology providers have identifiable opportunities in Vietnam's energy transition, supported by EDC's new Vietnam office.
🔗
Vietnam Semiconductor & High-Tech Manufacturing FDI Programme
Vietnam's government is aggressively attracting semiconductor packaging, electronics manufacturing, and high-tech industrial FDI. The JETP financing framework and Vietnam's National Semiconductor Strategy create procurement and partnership opportunities for Canadian technology companies in manufacturing equipment, precision engineering, environmental monitoring, and workforce training. The Canada-Vietnam Joint Economic Committee (JEC) is the bilateral mechanism for government-facilitated commercial introductions.
🔗
TCS Hanoi & Ho Chi Minh City — CanExport & EDC Support
TCS Vietnam's two offices provide Canadian companies with procurement intelligence, partner introductions, regulatory navigation support, and advocacy. EDC's Ho Chi Minh City office (opened March 2024) specifically supports Canadian exporters and investors accessing Vietnam's fast-growing market. CanExport SME funding covers eligible Vietnam market development expenses.

Government Signals

📄
Minister Ng Team Canada Trade Mission to Vietnam · March 2024
Canada's Minister of Export Promotion Mary Ng led a Team Canada Trade Mission to Vietnam in March 2024, bringing over 135 Canadian businesses and organizations. The mission celebrated the fifth anniversary of CPTPP's entry into force in Vietnam, announced the opening of EDC's first Vietnam office, and reinforced Vietnam as a priority market under Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy. The mission created direct commercial introductions across agri-food, cleantech, ICT, and education sectors.
📄
Canada-Vietnam Joint Economic Committee (JEC) · Established January 2022
The Canada-Vietnam JEC was established in January 2022 and held its inaugural meeting in Vancouver in July 2022. Co-chaired at the Deputy Minister level, the JEC is the primary bilateral mechanism for addressing trade and commercial matters, monitoring CPTPP implementation, and identifying new bilateral cooperation opportunities. The JEC's existence signals both governments' commitment to actively managing the commercial relationship rather than relying on CPTPP provisions alone.
📄
Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy — Vietnam Priority Market · November 2022
Canada's Indo-Pacific Strategy identifies Vietnam as a priority market within ASEAN, citing CPTPP partnership, bilateral trade growth, and the strategic importance of deepening Canada's Southeast Asia footprint. The strategy commits enhanced TCS resources and EDC presence to the Vietnam market — commitments acted on with TCS staffing increases and EDC's 2024 office opening. Vietnamese engagement is framed as both bilateral and as an ASEAN gateway.
📄
CPTPP Fifth Anniversary Review & Vietnam Implementation · 2024
The fifth anniversary of CPTPP's entry into force in Vietnam (January 2024) prompted a bilateral review of implementation progress and remaining barriers. Vietnam had eliminated approximately 66% of tariff lines for Canadian imports as of 2024, with continued phase-out of agricultural tariffs scheduled. Canada's 94% tariff elimination for Vietnamese goods is substantially complete. The Canada-Vietnam JEC provides a bilateral escalation channel for outstanding market access issues.
